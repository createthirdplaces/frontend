How economic distortions incentivize large construction projects over housing and public amenities

{{>authors/articleHeading name='Bharat Ponnaluri' date='2026' imageLink='https://createthirdplaces.nyc3.cdn.digitaloceanspaces.com/people/Bharat_Picture.jpg' }} Abandoned shopping center in Ohio. By Mike Kalasnik from Fort Mill, USA - Wal-Mart City View Center Cleveland, OH 2Uploaded by AlbertHerring, CC BY-SA 2.0, Link

Cities need to continually develop in order to accomodate more people and adapt to changes. However, distorted incentives contribute to large buildings that do not effectively meet the needs of residents.

These large developments lock out smaller developers who have limited cash flow and borrowing power. They also raise the cost of land, which increases property taxes for residents and makes housing more expensive. In addition, they magnify economic distortions that cause long term instability.

Addressing the distortions will reduce the number of unnecessary large construction projects and improve economic stability. Encouraging smaller developments will make development accessible to more people. This promotes resiliency that will mitigate risks from economic disruptions.

The problems with unnecessary large building construction

Construction of unnecessary large buildings in a neighborhood will magnify the contributing economic distortions and create a self-reinforcing cycle. In the short term, quality of life will be affected. Residents will have to pay more in rent or property taxes, leaving them with less spending money. The costs may also push out some residents. Meanwhile, moving to the neighborhood will become more expensive, which will prevent more people from accessing the benefits of the area.

Meanwhile, amenities are also likely to be neglected, as they are not suitable for investments. The public amenities that are created become tools to draw investment and will start being ignored when they are no longer helping to increase property values. On the other hand, developments focused on proving value to a neighborhood such as a mid-rise apartment, then the building will be as large as necessary. These buildings will minimize distortions from outside investment, and attract less nearby speculative investment that drives up housing prices. Meanwhile, density with low and mid-rise buildings promote bottom up development that makes neighborhoods more resilient.

Large buildings often remain partially or entirely empty for years and use space that could become apartments or community amenities. Meanwhile, developers are likely to keep rents at inflated values, which makes it harder for small business and residents to use the space.

This can be seen by the construction of a high rise in Rosslyn, Arlington called 1812 North Moore to speculate on the economic recovery from the 2007-2009 Great Recession. After building construction finished in 2013, it remained vacant until 2017 when Nestlé moved its US headquarters there. Meanwhile, Rosslyn's office vacancy rate tripled from around 10% to more than 31% between 2011 and 2014. In 2015, the office vacancy rate across Arlington was at a historic high of 21%. However, as of the end of 2015, the developer did not decrease the rents. The developer's CEO also mentioned that floor-by-floor deals for smaller businesses would affect the value of other buildings in Rosslyn.

Sometimes, large buildings end up reinforcing inefficient land use. One example is single story buildings for big-box stores with large surface parking lots. It is expensive for local goverments to maintain infrastructure neccesary to support big-box stores, and they prevent development of multi-story buildings in urban areas. They also take up space that could be used for housing or other community ammenities. Also, if a retail tenant leaves, it is expensive to redevelop the space.

For example, there is a large shopping center called the Potomac Yard Center in Alexandria that is close to half a mile long and contains multiple bix box stores.The southern end is within close walking distance of the Potomac Yard Metro station.

Potomac Yard Center

Sometimes, developers will close or demolish a building to prepare for redevelopment. Then the redevelopment will stop and the developer will end up sitting on a property. One example is an empty site in Maryland near the North Bethesda Metro station which used to be White Flint Mall. The developer closed the mall in 2015 and later demolished it as part of a redevelopment plan.

After White Flint Mall was demolished, the redevelopment stopped. As of April 2026, the site of the mall was empty. White Flint Mall had flaws and was overdue for redevelopment. However, a mall is a better use of land compared to empty space. Former site of White Flint Mall in April 2026(Photo by Bharat Ponnaluri)

Economic distortions from unnecessary large building construction can cause a real estate bubble to form. When the bubble pops, there will be widespread economic turmoil. Jobs and small businesses will be affected, and local governments will face lower tax revenues. Meanwhile, the neighborhood can't rely on help from residents who were pushed out due to high prices. Successful and resilient neighborhoods rely on residents who are familiar with the area and know other residents they can work with. Meanwhile, people are likely to move out if they lose their job and need a cheaper place to live.

In the case where rent goes down significantly after a bubble pop, long term problems can occur. Meanwhile lower rents will still be an issue for people who lost their job. Declining rents contribute to declining property values. This is a problem for local governments which rely on property taxes to fund essential services. The can be a sustained decline as a deterioration of essential services causes further property value decreases. Meanwhile, poverty rates will increase as jobs disappear. In Detroit a decades long decline led to the city losing more than half its population, and bankruptcy in 2013.

An analysis of the 2007 property bubble collapse

The collapse of a property bubble in 2007 demonstrates the risk of distorted investments. The bubble collapse in 2007, triggered a global financial crisis, and a major recession known as the Great Recession.

During the Great Recession, urban housing costs continued to increase. According to data form the US Bureau of Labor Statistics, the cost of housing in urban areas went up by 3.38% during the recession, while inflation was only 1.58%. In the New York City area, the cost of housing went up by 3.93%, despite being the epicenter of a global financial crisis triggered by the bubble collapse.

One cause of the 2007 bubble collapse was commercial mortgage backed securities(CMBS). Investors also used high leverage to turn a small amount of debt into a large investment. This meant small defaults turned into significantly higher losses. CMBS securities and high leverage debt investments are still used today and are a risk factor. They can be misused as a way to incentivize unnecessary large buildings. The ability to make money from CMBS debt for building construction can be seen as more valuable than making sure the building is useful.

Why developers construct large buildings.

It's important to understand why large buildings are constructed when they don't reflect the reality of the neighborhood. This will help make sure buildings are constructed to meet the needs of current and future residents.

Reduced risk of constructing large buildings

Large buildings have the potential to generate more money, and developers have ways of mitigating risk. They can sit on empty buildings without much cost. Also, a developer can gamble on being able to sell an unused building.

Real estate investment trusts

Large developers can form a real estate investment trust(REIT), which exempts them from corporate taxation. Large developers are incentivized to create large buildings because of higher potential profits and risk mitigation, and REITs give them an additional competitive advantage over smaller developers building low to mid rise density. Even if a smaller developer was able to form a REIT, it would be harder for them to attract investment because they get less attention and are seen as an unknown risk.

REITs are also designed to attract investments, regardless of whether their buildings provide value to residents. Also, REIT investors are likely to not live in a neighborhood where a REIT owns property, which makes it hard for them to evaluate whether a property improves the quality of life in a neighborhood.

REITs also promote investment in large buildings, which becomes an incentive to construct more of them. An investor evaluating a REIT will have an easier time evaluating one with 20 story buildings over one that has twice as many 10 story buildings.

More profits from government infrastructure improvements

Developers of large buildings benefit from the construction of improvements created by local governments in the form of increased property values. Policies such as tax increment financing to make a developer help pay for the costs improvements are not always implemented. Meanwhile, infrastructure improvements for a smaller project are probably going to be far less significant, which limits their effect on improving property values.

The ability to attract nearby development that will increase property value value

Large buildings also change the market by attracting other nearby development. This drives up property values Meanwhile, a smaller development isn't going to have such a large effect.

Financing challenges

Smaller developers building low to mid rise density have a hard time getting loans from banks. As a result, competition is reduced.

Recommendations

Promote civic engagement

Adam's Morgan Day in 2017 By S Pakhrin from DC, USA - Adam's Morgan Day 2017, CC BY 2.0, Link

Civic engagement encourages residents to give feedback on neighborhood development. Local governments can then use that feedback to support improvements that improve the quality of life in a neighborhood while reducing unnecessary construction of large buildings. Civic engagement also has the added benefit of guiding construction of infrastructure.

One example of civic engagement can be seen with Arlington County, Virginia gathering feedback from residents about development of the Rosslyn-Ballston corridor. In the 1960s, Arlington County worked with residents to make Metro's Orange Line run underground from Rosslyn to Ballston instead of in a highway median. Continued civic engagement and the Orange Line turned the Rosslyn-Ballston corridor from a suburb into a thriving urban area.

Local governments should also support community spaces such as parks and libraries. Through these spaces, residents will form connections that promote civic engagement.

Split-rate taxation where land is taxed at a higher rate

Split-rate taxation reduces incentives for developers to sit on vacant property. This makes it harder for buy land as a speculative investment. They will also have less of an incentive to build a high rise to attract nearby developments or infrastructure improvements to increase land value.

Developers will also be incentivized to sell property they can't develop, which gives more opportunities for local developers. Also, higher land value taxes will incentivize the development of underdeveloped or empty space, which will reduce the demand for high rises. In urban areas, it is common for high rises to be near space that is empty. {{>authors/bharatPonnaluri}}
