How economic distortions incentivize large construction projects over housing and public amenities

By Mike Kalasnik from Fort Mill, USA - Wal-Mart City View Center Cleveland, OH 2Uploaded by AlbertHerring, CC BY-SA 2.0, Link

Cities need to develop in order to accomodate more people and adapt to changes. However, distorted incentives contribute to large buildings that do not effectively meet the needs of residents.

These large buildings lock out smaller developers who have limited cash flow and borrowing power. They also raise the cost of land, which increases property taxes for residents and makes housing more expensive. Also, they contribute to long term instability.

Addressing the distortions will ensure that new building construction benefits current and future residents while improving economic stability. Smaller developers accessible to local developers will also be encouraged. As a result, neighborhoods will be more resilient.

The problems with unnecessary large building construction

Construction of unnecessary large buildings in a neighborhood will magnify economic distortions and create a self-reinforcing cycle that encourages more unnecessary large building. In the short term, quality of life will likely be affected. Residents will have to pay more in rent or property taxes, leaving them with less spending money. The costs may also push out some resident and prevent people from moving in.

Meanwhile, amenities might also be neglected because they are not suitable as investments. For example, in the Crystal City neighborhood of Arlington, Virginia, the local civic association asked for a new library. However, the one developer JBG Smith that owned suitable space for a library successfully cancelled a commitment to Arlington to build one. Meanwhile, JBG Smith has more than 100,000 square feet of empty space in Crystal City. If a large developer creates public amenities, they are likely to become tools to draw investment, and will start being ignored when they are no longe useful to increase property values.

JBG Smith has been prioritizing large building construction and mentions affordability as a business risk. In their 2025 annual report filed with the SEC, JBG Smith said: "Increased affordability of residential homes and other competition for tenants of our multifamily properties could affect our ability to retain current residents of our multifamily properties, attract new ones or increase or maintain rents, which could adversely affect our results of operations and our financial condition".

Section of a Crystal City public park that was converted into a luxury restaurant

On the other hand, developments focused on proving value to a neighborhood such as mid-rise apartments are less likely to be unnecessarily large. They will also minimize distortions from outside investment, which includes nearby speculative investment that drives up housing prices. Meanwhile, bottom-up development that makes neighborhoods more resilient is more likely to be developed.

Large buildings often remain empty for years. Meanwhile, developers are likely to keep rents at inflated values if they chose the make the space available. This prevents use by small businesses and residents

This can be seen a high rise in Rosslyn, Arlington called 1812 North Moore that was to speculate on the economic recovery from the 2007-2009 Great Recession. After construction finished in 2013, the building remained vacant until 2017 when Nestlé moved its US headquarters there. Meanwhile, Rosslyn's office vacancy rate tripled from around 10% to more than 31% between 2011 and 2014. In 2015, the office vacancy rate across Arlington was at a historic high of 21%. However, as of the end of 2015, the developer did not decrease the rents. The developer's CEO also mentioned that floor-by-floor deals for smaller businesses would affect the value of other nearby buildings.

Empty 1812 North Moore Building (By AgnosticPreachersKid - Own work, CC BY-SA 4.0, Link

Large buildings can also reinforcing inefficient land use. One example is single story buildings for big-box stores with large surface parking lots. It is expensive for local governments to maintain infrastructure neccessary to support big-box stores, and they take up space that could be used for housing or other community amenities. Also, if a retail tenant leaves, it is expensive to redevelop the space.

For example, there is a shopping center called the Potomac Yard Center in Alexandria that is close to half a mile long and contains multiple big-box stores. The southern end is within close walking distance of the Potomac Yard Metro station.

Potomac Yard Center

Developers might close or demolish a large building to prepare for redevelopment. Then developers can sit on a property if redevelopment stops. One example is an empty site in Maryland near the North Bethesda Metro station which used to be White Flint Mall. The developer closed the mall in 2015 and later demolished it as part of a redevelopment plan.

After White Flint Mall was demolished, the redevelopment stopped. As of April 2026, the site of the mall was empty. White Flint Mall had flaws and was overdue for redevelopment. However, a mall is a better use of land compared to empty space. Former site of White Flint Mall in April 2026(Photo by Bharat Ponnaluri)

Distortions from unnecessary large building construction can cause a real estate bubble to form. When the bubble pops, jobs and small businesses will be affected, and local governments will face lower tax revenues. Meanwhile, the neighborhood can't rely on help from residents who were pushed out due to high prices. Successful and resilient neighborhoods rely on residents who are familiar with the area and know other residents they can work with. People are likely to move out if they lose their job and need a cheaper place to live.

2009 picture of abandoned Packard Automotive Plant in Detroit. By Albert duce - Own work, CC BY-SA 3.0, Link

The pop of a bubble can cause lasting problems. The decline of property values will cause problems for local governments which rely on property taxes to fund essential services. A negative spiral could start as a deterioration of essential services cause further property value decreases. Meanwhile, poverty rates will increase as jobs disappear. In Detroit a decades long decline led to the city losing more than half its population, and bankruptcy in 2013.

2009 picture of Michigan Central Station in Detroit. By Rick Harris from Toronto, Ontario, Canada - Michigan Central Station [3], CC BY-SA 2.0, Link

An analysis of the 2007 property bubble collapse

The collapse of a property bubble in 2007 demonstrates the risk of distorted investments. The bubble collapse triggered a global financial crisis, and a major recession known as the Great Recession.

During the Great Recession, urban housing costs continued to increase. According to data from the US Bureau of Labor Statistics, housing costs in urban areas went up by 3.38% during the recession, while inflation was only 1.58%. In the New York City area, the cost of housing went up by 3.93%, despite being the epicenter of the global financial crisis.

Why developers construct unnecessary large buildings

It's important to understand why unnecessary large buildings are constructed despite their issues. This will help make sure buildings are constructed to meet the needs of current and future residents.

Reduced risk of constructing large buildings

Large buildings have the potential to generate more money, and developers have ways of mitigating risk. They can sit on empty buildings without much cost. Also, a developer can gamble on being able to sell an unused building.

Real estate investment trusts

Large developers can form a real estate investment trust(REIT) to avoid corporate taxation. Large developers are incentivized to create large buildings because of higher potential profits and risk mitigation, and REITs give them an additional competitive advantage over local developers building low to mid-rise buildings. Even if a smaller developer was able to form a REIT, it would be harder for them to attract investment because they get less attention and are seen as an unknown risk.

REITs are also designed to attract investments, regardless of whether their buildings provide value to residents. Also, REIT investors are likely to not live in a neighborhood where a REIT owns property, which makes it hard for them to evaluate whether a property improves the quality of life in a neighborhood.

REITs also promote investment in large buildings, which becomes an incentive to construct more of them. An investor evaluating a REIT will have an easier time evaluating one with 20 story buildings over one that has twice as many 10 story buildings.

More profits from government infrastructure improvements

Developers of large buildings benefit from the construction of improvements created by local governments with increased property values. Policies such as tax increment financing to make a developer help pay for the costs improvements are not always implemented. Meanwhile, infrastructure improvements for a smaller project are probably going to be far less significant, which limits their effect on property values.

The ability to attract nearby development that will increase property value value

Large buildings also change the market by attracting other nearby development. This drives up property values Meanwhile, a smaller development isn't going to have such a large effect.

Financing challenges

Smaller developers have a harder time getting loans from banks. As a result, large developers have less competition.

Recommendations

Promote civic engagement

Adam's Morgan Day in 2017 By S Pakhrin from DC, USA - Adam's Morgan Day 2017, CC BY 2.0, Link

Civic engagement encourages residents to give feedback on neighborhood development. Local governments can then use that feedback to support improvements that improve a neighborhood while reducing unnecessary construction of large buildings. Civic engagement also has the added benefit of guiding construction of infrastructure.

One example of civic engagement can be seen with Arlington, Virginia gathering feedback from residents about development of the Rosslyn-Ballston corridor. In the 1960s, Arlington worked with residents to make Metro's Orange Line run underground from Rosslyn to Ballston instead of in a highway median. Continued civic engagement and the Orange Line turned the Rosslyn-Ballston corridor from a suburb into a thriving urban area.

Rosslyn-Ballston corridor. (Photo by Arlington County)

Local governments should also support community spaces such as parks and libraries. Through these spaces, residents will form connections that promote civic engagement.

Split-rate taxation where land is taxed at a higher rate

Split-rate taxation reduces the incentive to construct large buildings as a speculative bet on making money from a land value increase. Higher land value taxes will also incentivize the development of underdeveloped or empty space. Developers will be incentivized to sell unused property they do not develop, which gives opportunities for local developers.
