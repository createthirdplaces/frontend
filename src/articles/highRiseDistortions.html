High rises are common in cities. Developers build high rises because it is economically viable, with the cost of land in cities being the main factor. However, the viability of high rises across most urban areas in the US is based on economic distortions.

The construction of high rises is making housing more expensive. They also make it harder for smaller local developers to participate due to the cost of high rises. Too many high rises also magnify economic distortions that cause long term problems

Why developers build too many high rises

There are several significant factors incentivizing developers to build high rises. They are based on market distorions and should be addressed.

Reduced risk of buidling high rises

Developers can sit on empty buildings without much cost. Also, a developer can gamble on being able to sell an unused building.

REIT taxation

The ability to profit off government infrastructure improvements

The ability to attract nearby development that will increase land value

Inflated land values

Tall high rise construction can be justified in a moment by high land prices, but those high land prices are often artificial distortions

An analysis of the 2008 financial cricis and its impact on urban areas

Recommended solutions

Split-rate taxation where land is taxed at a higher rate

Split-rate taxation would prevent developers from sitting on a vacant property. This makes it harder for them to use land as a speculative investment. As a result, they will have less of an incentive to build a high rise to attract nearby developments or infrastructure improvements that make their land more valuable.

Developers will also be more incentivzed to sell property they can't develop, which gives more opportunities for local developers. Also, higher land value taxes will incentivize the development of underdeveloped or empty space, which will reduce the demand for high rises. In urban areas, it is common for high rises to be near space that is empty.

Minimize adding strict rules such as height limits.
