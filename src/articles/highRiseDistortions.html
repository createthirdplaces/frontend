How economic distortions incentivize tall buildings over housing and public amenities

{{>authors/articleHeading name='Bharat Ponnaluri' date='2026' imageLink='https://createthirdplaces.nyc3.cdn.digitaloceanspaces.com/people/Bharat_Picture.jpg' }}

Development is essential to make sure cities can accomodate more people and adapt to changes. However, distorted incentives artificially boost the construction of large private developments, especially high rises.

These high rises make housing more expensive and lock out smaller developers who can't afford to buy a high rise to participate. They also raise the cost of land. In addition, they magnify economic distortions that cause instability

On the other hand, fixing the economic distortions will improve economic stability by reducing the unnecessary large construction projects. Diversity in property ownership by incentivizing small developers will also promote resiliency that will mitigate the effects of real estate bubbles.

The problems with unnecessary high rise construction

The economic distortions that contribute to too much high rise construction also magnify the economic distortions, creating a self-reinforcing cycle, which affects quality of life. The rise in property values causes property taxes and rents to go up, which means residents have less money to spend. Some residents will be pushed out due to the high costs. Meanwhile, it will become more expensive to move into the area, which will prevent more people from accessing the benefits of an area.

Often large buildings will remain partially or entirely empty for years. Meanwhile, the buildings take up space that could be used for apartments or community amenities. Rents are also likely to not decline from inflated values, which makes it harder for small business and residents to use the space.

This can be seen by the construction of a high rise in Rosslyn, Arlington called 1812 North Moore to speculate on the economic recovery from the 2007-2009 recession. Even as the local job market declined again, construction still continued.

After construction of the building finished in 2013, it remained vacant until 2017 Nestlé moved its US headquarters to the building. Meanwhile, Rosslyn's office vacancy rate tripled from around 10% to more than 31% between 2011 and 2014. In 2015, the office vacancy rate across Arlington was at a historic high of 21%. However, as of the end of 2015, the developer did not decrease the rents. The developer's CEO also mentioned that floor-by-floor deals for smaller businesses would affect the value of other buildings in Rosslyn.

Public amenities are also likely to be neglected, as they are not suitable for investments. The public amenities that are created become ways of drawing in more investment and will start being ignored when they are no longer helping to increase property values. On the other hand, if development is focused on proving value to a neighborhood such as by building a mid rise apartment, then the building will be as large as it needs to be. This minimizes distortions from outside investment, and will attract less nearby speculative investment that drives up housing prices.

If the cycle is not stopped a major real estate bubble can form, and cause widespread economic turmoil. Jobs and small businesses will be affected, and local governments will be facing lower tax revenues. Recovery will be challenging because residents familiar with the neighborhood were pushed out due to high prices. Successful and resilient neighborhoods rely on local residents who are familiar with the area and know other residents they can work with. Meanwhile, people who moved in are likely to move out if they lose their job and need a cheaper place to live.

Housing prices in cities are also likely to not decline when a bubble collapses. This was seen during the property bubble collapse in 2007, which caused the worst economic crisis in the US since the Great Depression. The US was in a recession from December 2007 to June 2009. During this time, the US Bureau of Labor Statistics found that the cost of housing in urban areas went up by 3.38% while inflation was only 1.58%.

One cause of the 2007 bubble collapse was commercial mortgage backed securities(CMBS). Investors also used high leverage to turn a small amount of debt into a large investment. This meant small defaults turned into significantly higher losses. CMBS securities and high leverage debt investments are still used today and are a risk factor. They can be misused as a way to incentivize unnecessary large buildings. The ability to make money of debt for building construction is seen as more valuable than making sure the building is useful.

Meanwhile, density with low and mid rise buildings promote bottom up development that makes neighborhoods more resilient to bubbles.

Why developers build too many high rises

When there is a rapid increase in property values, it's important to understand why. If the increase in prices doesn't reflect in increase in demand, then action should be taken to bring prices back to reality. This will make it easier for people to pay for housing and reduce the impact from a property bubble pop like the one that was seen in 2008.

Reduced risk of building high rises

Real estate developers also have ways of mitigating the risk of building high rise building. They can sit on empty buildings without much cost. Also, a developer can gamble on being able to sell an unused building.

Real estate investment trusts

Large developers can form a real estate investment trust(REIT), which exempts them from corporate taxation, while it is difficult for a smaller developer to become a REIT. Large developers are incentivized to create large buildings because of higher potential profits and risk mitigation, and REITs give them an additional competitive advantage over smaller developers building low to mid rise density. Even if a smaller developer was able to form a REIT, it would be harder for them to attract investment because they get less attention and are seen as an unknown risk.

REITs are also designed to attract investments that are looking for financial returns, regardless of whether their buildings provide value to residents of a neighborhood. Also, REIT investors are likely to not live in a neighborhood where a REIT owns property, which makes it hard for them to evaluate whether a property improves the quality of life in a neighborhood.

REITs also promote investment in large buildings, which becomes an incentive to construct more of them. An investor trying to evaluate a REIT will have an easier time evaluating one with 20 story buildings over one that has twice as many 10 story buildings.

The ability to profit off government infrastructure improvements

Builders of tall high rises will also benefit from the construction of public transit infrastructure and facilities built by local governments such as libraries and community centers. They make a neighborhood more desirable, which increases land and property values. Policies such as tax increment financing to make a developer help pay for the cost of these improvements are not always implemented. Meanwhile, infrastructure investments for a smaller project are probably going to be far less modest, which limits their effect on improving property values.

The ability to attract nearby development that will increase land value

A tall high rises also change the market by attracting other nearby development. This drives up property values for the developer who built the high rise. Meanwhile, a smaller development isn't going to have such a large effect on the market and will get a smaller value bump from the amount of nearby development it attracts.

Financing challenges

Smaller developers building low to mid rise density have a hard time getting loans from banks. As a result, competition is reduced.

Inflated land values

Tall high rise construction can be justified in a moment by high land prices, but those high land prices are often artificial distortions.

Recommendations

Promote civic engagement

Civic engagement will encourage residents to give more feedback on how their neighborhoods should be developed. Local governments can then use that feedback to support buildings that improve the quality of life in a neighborhood while reducing unnecessary construction of tall buildings. Civic engagement also has the added benefit of guiding the construction of infrastructure that improves a neighborhood.

One prominent example of civic engagement can be seen with Arlington County, Virginia gathering feedback from residents to determine how the Rosslyn-Ballston corridor should be developed. In the 1960s, Arlington County also worked with residents to make Metro's Orange Line run underground from Rosslyn to Ballston instead of in a highway median. Continued civic engagement and the Orange Line turned the Rosslyn-Ballston corridor from a suburb into a thriving urban area.

Local governments should also support community spaces such as parks and libraries. Through these spaces, residents will form connections that promote civic engagement.

Split-rate taxation where land is taxed at a higher rate

Split-rate taxation would prevent developers from sitting on a vacant property. This makes it harder for them to use land as a speculative investment. As a result, they will have less of an incentive to build a high rise to attract nearby developments or infrastructure improvements that make their land more valuable.

Developers will also be more incentivized to sell property they can't develop, which gives more opportunities for local developers. Also, higher land value taxes will incentivize the development of underdeveloped or empty space, which will reduce the demand for high rises. In urban areas, it is common for high rises to be near space that is empty.

Do not impose height limits in high density areas.

While height limits will reduce the effect of distortions from high rises, new height limits should not be added. The optimal building height will change over time, and will not be the same across across a region. Trying to determine an optimal building height for each neighborhood and updating will be a very time consuming task and there is no guarantee that the results will be accurate. Strict building height limits can also be an issue when building a tall building might be useful such as quickly building housing for a large number of people.

Despite the fact that high rise construction is often the result of market distortions, there are cases when constructing them makes sense. Sometimes, the building is in a very desirable location with expensive land, which makes tall buildings more cost-effective.

On the other hand, if a city already has strict height limits such as DC, while allowing mid rise density, then they shouldn't be removed. While strict height limits like the ones across DC are not ideal, removing the height limits would cause disruption because developers will have to adapt to different rules. {{>authors/bharatPonnaluri}}
