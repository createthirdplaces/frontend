Crystal City, Virginia and the limits of master-planned urbanism for supporting public space

{{>authors/articleHeading name='Bharat Ponnaluri' date='April 18, 2026' imageLink='https://createthirdplaces.nyc3.cdn.digitaloceanspaces.com/people/Bharat_Picture.jpg' }}

Crystal City, Virginia is often held up as a model for large-scale transit-oriented development. However billions of dollars in investment and decades of planning have struggled to create public spaces where people want to gather.

Crystal City shows how master-planned projects created with good intentions fail to create "public spaces", and why bottom-up action is important for creating and maintaining local public spaces.

How Crystal City become locked into a 40 year plan

Large scale development started in Crystal City in the 1960s by the Charles E. Smith company, which is now part of JBG Smith. Eventually, that landlord would end up owning most of the non-residential property in Crystal City. They were quoted as saying "How many men get to start with a blank sheet of paper and create a city?". It took years for the landlord to realize they had built a community and attempt to make community amenities.

In the 1970s, the landlord built a large underground mall known as the Crystal City Underground or the Underground with over a hundred retailers and a variety of restaurants. While the mall did encourage some bottom up action by supporting small business tenants, their existence was based on a shaky foundation of traffic from office workers. Poor visibility from the outside, and limited outdoor walkability due to large blocks were issues. A large stroad known as Route 1 also made walking more difficult, especially after part of the stroad was converted into a highway.

In the early 2000s, BRAC caused Crystal City to lose many of its Federal Government tenants, and the 2008 recession caused further losses. Stores in the Underground started to close as they did not have enough foot traffic from remaining office workers and residents.

Arlington County hired an urban planning firm Torti Galls + Partners to create an updated plan for Crystal City. The plan was adopted in 2010 as the Crystal City Sector Plan. It was seen as a 40 year blueprint for how Crystal City would be developed.

This sector plan emphasized a focus on large developments with buildings up to 300 feet tall, 20,000 jobs, and 14,000 new residents in an area that is approximately 0.4 square miles. Recent high rise developments have demonstrated that the plan is being followed. At the same time, input from locals has been ignored, and empty space remains undeveloped.

The requirements of the sector plan incentivize high density developments that cost millions of dollars. Most of the new development has also been done by one real estate company, JBG Smith, which has allowed them to maintain a virtual monopoly on most of Crystal City. Also, there isn't a process to modify the 40 year sector plan. These factors have caused significant challenges for bottom up incremental change.

Empty land in Crystal City next to high rise buildings.

When Community Emerges and Then Gets Pushed Out

While large developments were prioritized, bottom up action successfully created public spaces in the Crystal City Underground. Unfortunately, actions by the landlord JBG Smith interfered with the success of these spaces.

The Landing

Locals started to host informal events at a space in the Underground called The Landing. Eventually, there would be around 100 people at The Landing on Friday nights between the board game nights I hosted and other groups. People would also often meet there on evenings and weekends.

Unfortunately, when JBG Smith removed the tables and chairs in 2024, we didn't have any recourse. They did not communicate clear plans for the Underground and was unwilling to work with community members to support alternative public spaces in Crystal City. Small businesses in the Underground were also forced to close around the same time.

The Landing in 2026 after tables and chairs were removed. (Photo by Bharat Ponnaluri)

When I lived in Crystal City, I enjoyed being able to walk to The Landing and meet people there. The Landing also encouraged a culture of self organizing and community ownership, which was reflected in the board game events I attended there. When an official host wasn't able to be there, other regulars including myself would make sure things ran smoothly. This helped me when I become an official host.

In 2016, Arlington County opened a pop-up library in the Underground after getting input from local residents. Local advocacy contributed to the library staying open until 2019, although the original plan was to keep it open for 9 months. This included the Crystal City Civic Association making a formal request to the county, and individuals reaching out to the Arlington County Board.

Connection Library in 2017. (Source: Wikimedia Commons)

Unfortunately, the Connection closed in December 2019, because decreased attendance caused Arlington County to decide paying for library staffing was not a priority. A restaurant and makerspace nearby closed, which caused foot traffic to decrease. JBG Smith did not work with Connection staff to draw additional foot traffic or move the library to another available space for rent.

Hallway with vacant space. The glass doors on the right were entrances to the Connection library. (Photo by Bharat Ponnaluri)

In 2022, JBG Smith agreed to build a new library as part of another development. However, JBG Smith was able to cancel the library plans by paying Arlington $5.8 million.

The Financial Case for Bottom-Up Growth in Arlington County

Community spaces make people more invested in the quality of the neighborhoods, which helps promote bottom up action. Also, the spaces help bring people together and form partnerships to make small neighborhood improvements.

The same partnerships help people work together to form small businesses, which create jobs and increase local wealth. The localized investment will be more stable than income from a large real estate company that doesn't share the interests of residents. The success of smaller localized investments can be seen from 23rd Street in Crystal City.

23rd Street restaurants in Crystal City.

On the other hand, Arlington County has become increasingly dependent on large-scale development for property taxes, and that has caused revenue challenges. In fiscal year(FY) 2025 and FY 2026, 56% of Arlington County's tax revenue came from property taxes, and that number is forecast to go up to 57%. Meanwhile, the county's proposed $1.69 billion FY 2027 budget is slightly smaller than the previous year, reflecting rising costs and slow revenue growth.

Official Arlington County assessments show the challenge. In January 2026, property values increased by only 1.1%, which was the third consecutive year of declining growth, which inflation reached 2.7% with the costs of many services rising even faster. Meanwhile, residents are paying more. The average tax and fee burden is projected to reach $9,253 in FY 2027, up 55% from FY 2017. Arlington County's FY 2027 budget plan will mean homeowners will pay an average of $9,253 in taxes and fees, which is up 55% from FY 2017. This means an increased dependence on large developments in Crystal City and other parts of the county for property tax revenue.

Crystal City should move away from an overreliance on top-down development towards a pattern that supports bottom-up growth. At the same time, any shift will need to account for the county's current dependence on property tax revenue.

Recommendations for Local Officials and Residents

If Crystal City is going to become a place where people actually want to spend time, both local officials and residents have a role to play. Small, incremental changes can make a big difference, but only if the system allows them.

1. Reconsider how taxes are structured to encourage productive land use.

Empty parking lot on a Thursday evening in Crystal City. (Photo by Bharat Ponnaluri)

Crystal City has indoor spaces that have been vacant for years, empty plots of land, and underutilized parking lots.

Property taxes in Arlington County are based on assessed market value. In practice, this penalizes owners who create, improve, or maintain their buildings. Property owners may leave spaces vacant or hold out for higher rents, since potential rental income is reflected in property values. Maintaining a high value is important to product the property's value as collateral and prevent banks from foreclosing.

For the landlord that owns the Crystal City Underground, JBG Smith, maintaining high property values is a necessity to avoid the risk of loan defaults. JBG Smith's debt obligations for the 3 years from the start of 2026 to the end of 2028 are approximately $1.759 billion. With JBG Smith's 2025 revenue of $479 million, it would take 3.67 years to earn a total of $1.759 billion. JBG Smith will need to refinance debt that they can't afford to pay. This refinancing will require high property values so that their property can be used as collateral.

Arlington County should move to increase tax rates on privately-created building values while increasing the rate on publicly-created land values, which is called a split-rate land value tax. A split-rate land value tax would encourage property owners to either put their spaces to productive use or sell them to someone who will. Arlington County already asses land and building values separately, so this change would be building off an existing system.

In March 2026, the Virginia Senate passed HB282, which allows several localities to adopt a split-rate land value tax. This shows that there is political support for Arlington to also ask for permission.

Over time, a split-rate land value tax could lower rents and reduce the need for large public spending on housing affordability. Arlington County's proposed FY 2027 budget includes $98.1 million for housing affordability.

(This article by Rick Rybeck gives some additional suggestions on how to advocate for changing the tax system.)

2. Use public spaces more often and make that use visible.

Regular activity in public spaces signals demand. When people gather consistently, it becomes harder for property owners and local officials to ignore the need for places to meet, linger, and connect.

3. Advocate for local community spaces in conversations.

Advocacy helps people realize that spaces they walk by are community spaces. I learned The Landing in the Crystal City Underground was a community space through a conversation with a friend. I had walked by the Landing earlier in the year, and did not realize it was a popular site because it was almost empty at that time of day.

Word of mouth is a powerful force here: conversations help people see what’s possible, and help build support for keeping these spaces active and accessible.

4. Support community pop-ups and parks in empty spaces.

Pop-ups, temporary parks, and informal gathering spaces can turn underused areas into something meaningful with relatively little investment.

These small interventions create opportunities for people to test ideas, build relationships, and gradually improve their neighborhood without waiting for large-scale redevelopment.
