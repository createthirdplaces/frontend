How the history of Crystal City, Virginia demonstrates the importance of bottom up action for supporting public spaces

{{>authors/bharatPonnaluriHeading}}

Crystal City is a neighborhood located close to Washington DC in Arlington County. The neighborhood demonstrates why bottom up action is important for creating and maintaining local public spaces.

Large scale development started in Crystal City in the 1960s by the Charles E. Smith company. Eventually, that landlord would end up owning most of the non-residential property in Crystal City. They were quoted as saying "How many men get to start with a blank sheet of paper and create a city?". It took years for the landlord to realize they had built a community and attempt to make community amenities.

In the 1970s, the landlord built a large underground mall known as the Crystal City Underground or the Underground with over a hundred retailers and a variety of restaurants. While the mall did encourage some bottom up action by supporting small business tenants, their existence was based on a shaky foundation of traffic from office workers. Poor visibility from the outside, and limited outdoor walkability due to large blocks also had an effect. A large stroad known as Route 1 also made walking more difficult, and a part of Route 1 was eventually converted into an elevated highway.

In the early 2000s, Crystal City started to lose many large office tenants. This started with a federal government proccess called BRAC that resulted in Crystal City losing almost a third of its workforce and 3 million square feet of office space. The 2008 recession increased the losses. Stores in the Underground started to close as they did not have enough sustainable foot traffic from remaining office workers and residents.

The owner of the Underground continued prioritizing top down development to make Crystal City a shopping and eating destination. More high rise apartment buildings and office towers were constructed.

At the same time, input from locals was ignored, and empty space remained undeveloped.

Arlington County hired an urban planning firm to create an updated plan for Crystal City and the plan took much of it's input from office tenants and the one dominant property owner. The plan was adopted in 2010 as the Crystal City Sector plan and became a 40 year blueprint for how Crystal City would change.

This sector plan emphasized a focus on large developments with buildings up to 300 feet tall, 20,000 jobs, and 14,000 new residents in an area that is approximiately 0.4 square miles. Recent changes have demonstrated that the plan is being followed. Due to the density required to meet the goals of the sector plan, smaller developers can't afford construction in Crystal City. Also, there isn't a process to modify the 40 year sector plan. These two factors have made bottom up incremental change challenging.

Community spaces in Crystal City created by bottom up action

While the landlord focused on large developments, bottom up action successfully created public spaces in the Crystal City Underground. Unfortunately, actions by the landlord interfered with the success of these spaces.

The Landing

Locals started to host informal events at a space in the Underground called The Landing. Eventually, there would be around 100 people at The Landing on Friday nights between the board game nights I hosted and other groups. People would also often meet there on evenings and weekends.

Unfortunately, when the landlord removed the tables and chairs, we didn't have any recourse. The landlord did not communicate clear plans for the Underground and was unwilling to work with community members to support alternative public spaces in Crystal City. Small businesses in the Underground were also forced to close around the same time.

Empty hallway in the Underground.

Even before tables and chairs were all removed from the Landing, the quality of the space was affected by the landlord's focus on large development projects. For example, some of the tables and chairs became damaged and unusable. The landlord said they had no plans to pay for replacements and ignored my offer to contribute to the costs with a fundraiser.

The Landing after tables and chairs were removed.

When I lived in Crystal City, The Landing was a great place to meet people. I become friends with two people who lived in my apartment building because we met at The Landing. I also enjoyed being able to easily walk there. The Landing also encouraged a culture of self organizing and community ownership, which was reflected in how events were run. When the host wasn't able to be there for game nights, other regulars including myself would make sure things ran smoothly. This helped me when I become the official host.

A popup library in the Underground called the Connection was another example of successful bottom up action. In 2016, Arlington County opened the popup library after getting input from neighborhood residents.

It was originally proposed to be open for 9 months. However, it stayed open until 2019, and local advocacy was a key factor. This included the Crystal City Civic Association(CCCA) making a formal request to the county, and individual requests to the Arlington County Board. CCCA president Carol Fuller said "The Library has become a focal point for Crystal City". I lived in Crystal City when the Connection was opened, and it was convenient to have a library very close to where I lived.

Unfortunately, the Connection closed in December 2019. Actions by the landlord caused library attendance to decrease, and Arlington County decided that paying for library staffing was not a priority. A restaurant and makerspace nearby closed, which decreased foot traffic. The landlord did not work with Arlington to draw additional foot traffice or move the library to another available space for rent. Due to the landlord's dominance of Crystal City real estate, Arlington did not have room to negotiate.

In 2022, the landlord agreed to build a new library as part of another development. However, the landlord was able to cancel the library plans by paying Arlington $5.8 million.

The Crystal City Water Park: An Example Of The Limitations of Top Down Development

Crystal City has an outdoor plaza called the Water Park, and it is owned by the same landlord that owns the Underground. In 2023, the landlord completed a renovation of the Water Park to convert it into an outdoor food court.

Community input was ignored for the Water Park renovation, and it shows when walking through. When I went there on a Friday evening around 7pm, the space was mostly empty. I also saw that some stalls no longer had food vendors. Seasonal cold weather, warm weather with humidity, rain, or snow limited the viability of the outdoor food court. An empty space without obstacles such as chairs and tables would have been cheaper, and far more usable as a park.

Reasons it is important to prioritize local community spaces

Community spaces make people more invested in the quality of the neighborhoods, which helps promote bottom up action. Also, community spaces help bring people together and form partnerships to make small neighborhood improvements. Bottom up action doesn't happen in a vacuum by isolated individuals.

Community spaces also help people work together to form small businesses, which create jobs and increase local wealth. This localized investment is more stable than depending on a single landlord that doesn't live nearby.

Crystal City has a long history of issues with top down development, and the existing state of top down development isn't helping with revenue. The $1.69 billion fiscal year(FY) 2027 spending plan proposed in Feburary 2026 is a fraction of a percent less than the previous year. In fiscal year (FY) 2025 and 2026, 56% of Arlington County's tax revenue came from property taxes, and that number is forecast to go up to 57% in FY 2026. Official county assements in January 2026 found that there was a 1.1% increase in property values, and yearly CPI inflation was 2%. Local residents will have challenges adjusting to new taxes. Arlington County's FY 2027 budget plan will mean homeowners will pay an average of $9,253 in taxes and fees, which is up 55% from FY 2017. Total inflation between January 2016 and January 2026 was approximately 37%.

Crystal City should move away from this unsustainable focus on top down development and more towards sustainable bottom up development.

On the other hand, Crystal City has an example of successful incremental development with restaurants on 23rd Street.

Recommendations for local officials and residents.

Actions by local residents can help ensure local community spaces are prioritized. First, people should usepublic spaces more often. Seeing activity in a public space will show elected officials and private businesses hat there is demand for public space.

Also, one should advocate more for community space in conversation. I learned about The Landing in Crystal City as a community space because a friend told me about a board game group that met there. I had walked by the Landing earlier in the year, and did not realize it was a community space because it was almost empty. This advocacy can compensate for a public space being in a location that is hard to notice.

23rd Street in Crystal City is an example of how incremental development can be successful. It is less dependent on external factors such as the price of office real estate, and it gives more opportunities for smaller locally owned businesses.

Second Arlington County should focus on spread out medium density development and work with nearby counties to promote medium density development. There are challenges with trying to do this that should be overcome.

Near Crystal City, there is a big box shopping center with a parking lot that is close to half a mile long. The southern end is around a 5 minute walk from a Metro station, and it is around 1.5 miles away from the Crystal City Metro station. This would be a great location for some development that is currently concentrated in Crystal City. However, the shopping center is currently owned by a single develooper, which is blocking opportunities for bottom up development. A similar pattern is repeated in many other nearby areas. As a result, if medium density development is promoted in Crystal City over more high rises, increasing density in other suitable locations would be a challenge.
