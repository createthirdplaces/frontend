How the history of Crystal City, Virginia demonstrates the importance of bottom up action for supporting public spaces

{{>authors/bharatPonnaluriHeading}}

Crystal City is a neighborhood located close to Washington DC in Arlington County. The neighborhood demonstrates why bottom up action is important for creating and maintaining local public spaces.

Large scale development started in Crystal City in the 1960s by the Charles E. Smith company. Eventually, that landlord would end up owning most of the non-residential property in Crystal City. They were quoted as saying "How many men get to start with a blank sheet of paper and create a city?". It took years for the landlord to realize they had built a community and attempt to make community amenities.

In the 1970s, the landlord built a large underground mall known as the Crystal City Underground or the Underground with over a hundred retailers and a variety of restaurants. While the mall did encourage some bottom up action by supporting small business tenants, their existence was based on a shaky foundation of traffic from office workers. Poor visibility from the outside, and limited outdoor walkability due to large blocks also effects. A large stroad known as Route 1 also made walking more difficult, especially after part of the stroad was converted into a highway.

In the early 2000s, Crystal City started to lose many large office tenants that employed more than a third of its workforce. Stores in the Underground started to close as they did not have enough foot traffic from remaining office workers and residents.

Arlington County hired an urban planning firm to create an updated plan for Crystal City. The plan was adopted in 2010 as the Crystal City Sector plan. It was seen as a 40 year blueprint for how Crystal City would be developed.

This sector plan emphasized a focus on large developments with buildings up to 300 feet tall, 20,000 jobs, and 14,000 new residents in an area that is approximately 0.4 square miles. Recent high rise developments have demonstrated that the plan is being followed. At the same time, input from locals has been ignored, and empty space remains undeveloped.

The requirements of the sector plan incentivize high density developments that cost millions of dollars. Also, there isn't a process to modify the 40 year sector plan. These two factors have caused significant challenges for bottom up incremental change.

Community spaces in Crystal City created by bottom up action

While large developments were prioritized, bottom up action successfully created public spaces in the Crystal City Underground. Unfortunately, actions by the landlord interfered with the success of these spaces.

The Landing

Locals started to host informal events at a space in the Underground called The Landing. Eventually, there would be around 100 people at The Landing on Friday nights between the board game nights I hosted and other groups. People would also often meet there on evenings and weekends.

Unfortunately, when the landlord removed the tables and chairs in 2024, we didn't have any recourse. The landlord did not communicate clear plans for the Underground and was unwilling to work with community members to support alternative public spaces in Crystal City. Small businesses in the Underground were also forced to close around the same time.

The Landing after tables and chairs were removed.

When I lived in Crystal City, I enjoyed being able to walk to The Landing and meet people there. The Landing also encouraged a culture of self organizing and community ownership, which was reflected in the board game events I attended there. When an official host wasn't able to be there, other regulars including myself would make sure things ran smoothly. This helped me when I become an official host.

In 2016, Arlington County opened a pop-up library in the Underground after getting input from local residents. Local advocacy contributed to the library staying open until 2019, although the original plan was to keep it open for 9 months. This included the Crystal City Civic Association making a formal request to the county, and individuals reaching out to the Arlington County Board.

Unfortunately, the Connection closed in December 2019, because decreased attendance caused Arlington County to decide paying for library staffing was not a priority. A restaurant and makerspace nearby closed, which caused foot traffic to decrease. The landlord did not work with Connection staff to draw additional foot traffic or move the library to another available space for rent.

Hallway with vacant space. The glass doors on the right were entrances to the Connection library.

In 2022, the landlord agreed to build a new library as part of another development. However, the landlord was able to cancel the library plans by paying Arlington $5.8 million.

The Crystal City Water Park: An Example Of The Limitations of Top Down Development

Crystal City has an outdoor plaza called the Water Park, and it is owned by the same landlord that owns the Underground. In 2023, the landlord completed a renovation of the Water Park to convert it into an outdoor food court.

Community input was ignored for the Water Park renovation, and it shows when walking through. When I went there on a Friday evening around 7pm, the space was mostly empty. I also saw that some stalls no longer had food vendors. Seasonal cold weather, warm weather with humidity, rain, or snow limited the viability of the outdoor food court. An empty space without obstacles such as chairs and tables would have been cheaper, and far more usable as a park.

Crystal City Water Park food court. The tall building in the background is luxury apartment building constructed by the same landlord. As of 3-4-2026, the lowest monthly rent for a 1 bedroom was $3,070.

Reasons it is important to prioritize local community spaces

Community spaces make people more invested in the quality of the neighborhoods, which helps promote bottom up action. Also, the spaces help bring people together and form partnerships to make small neighborhood improvements.

The same partnerships help people work together to form small businesses, which create jobs and increase local wealth. The localized investment will be more stable than income from a single landlord that doesn't live nearby. The success of smaller localized investments can be seen from 23rd Street in Crystal City.

23rd Street restaurants in Crystal City.

The focus on large developments in Crystal City is contributing to Arlington County's current revenue challenges. However, the county is still dependent on revenue from large developments.

In fiscal year(FY) 2025 and FY 2026, 56% of Arlington County's tax revenue came from property taxes, and that number is forecast to go up to 57%. The county's $1.69 billion FY 2027 spending plan proposed in February 2026 is a fraction of a percent less than the previous year. This reflects concerns that include increasing costs combined with slow revenue growth.

Official Arlington County assessments in January 2026 found that there was a 1.1% increase in property values. This represented the 3rd consecutive year of declining growth. Inflation was 2.7%, and costs for many services has risen faster than this rate. Local residents will have challenges adjusting to new taxes. Arlington County's FY 2027 budget plan will mean homeowners will pay an average of $9,253 in taxes and fees, which is up 55% from FY 2017. This means an increased dependence on large developments in Crystal City and other parts of the county for property tax revenue.

Crystal City should move away from this unsustainable focus on top down development and more towards sustainable bottom up development. However changes should consider the fact that Arlington County is currently getting most of its revenue from property taxes.

Recommendations for local officials and residents

Reconsider how taxes are determined to incentivize development of unused space

Empty parking lot on a Thursday evening in Crystal City.

Crystal City has indoor spaces that have been vacant for years, empty plots of land, and underutilized parking lots.

Property taxes in Arlington County are determined by an official assessment of the fair market value. This incentivizes property owners to leave spaces empty because property taxes represent an additional cost for improvements. Also, potential rental earnings are a part of the market value of a property. This causes property owners to charge higher rents to make their properties more valuable, even if it means leaving spaces empty and paying more in property tax.

Arlington County should move to increase tax rates on land and decrease tax rates on property. This would incentivize property owners to put spaces to productive use or sell the property. As a result, there would be more opportunities for local small businesses and residents to make small improvements. Arlington County already assesses land value and building value separately due to Virginia state code rules.

This will have the added benefit of decreasing rents, and reducing the amount of money Arlington County has to spend on housing affordability. The proposed FY 2027 budget includes $98.1 million to invest in housing affordability.

Use public spaces more often

Seeing activity in a public space will show elected officials and private businesses that there is demand for community space.

Advocate for local community spaces in conversations

Advocacy helps people realize that spaces they walk by are community spaces. I learned The Landing in the Crystal City Underground was a community space through a conversation with a friend. I had walked by the Landing earlier in the year, and did not realize it was a community space because it was almost empty.

Support community pop-ups and parks in empty space

Building a park or a popup space can be a great low cost way to convert empty space into community space.
