How the history of Crystal City, Virginia demonstrates the importance of bottom up action for supporting public spaces

{{>authors/bharatPonnaluriHeading}}

Crystal City is a neighborhood located close to Washington DC, and demonstrates why bottom up action is important for creating local public spaces. Crystal City also shows why bottom up action by locals is important to maintain quality public spaces.

Large scale development started in Crystal City in the 1960s by a landlord wanting to create their own neighborhood. Eventually, that landord would end up owning most of the non-residential property in Crystal City. They were quoted as saying "How many men get to start with a blank sheet of paper and create a city?". It took years for the landlord to realize they had built a community and attempt to make community ammenities.

In the 1970s, the landlord built a large underground mall called the Crystal City Underground with over a hundred retailers and a variety of restaurants. The mall did include many small businesses, and their existence was built on a shaky foundation of traffic from office workers. In addition, visibility was an issue, and outdoor walkablity in Crystal City was poor due to large blocks.

In the early 2000s, Crystal City started to lose many large federal goverment and other office tenants. The 2008 recession increased the losses, and stores in the Underground started to close. The stores did not have enough sustainable foot traffic from residents.

The dominant landlord for Crystal City decided to continue the emphasis on top down development and decided to focus on making Crystal City a shopping and eating destination, while ignoring input from locals. They also started constructing large high rise apartments and priorized trying to get large office tenants. The focus on top down development has continued to this day.

During this focus on top down development, major successes in creating community space came from bottom-up development Unfortunately, top down development from the dominant landlord had a negative effect on these spaces. They were not willing to work with community members to preserve these community spaces, and there was no competing property owner we could negoiate an agreement with.

Independent action by locals converted a space called the Landing in the Crystal City Underground that used to be a cornerstone of the local community until all the tables and chairs were removed. On Friday nights, there would be around 100 people at The Landing between the board game nights I hosted and other groups. The Landing was also frequently used by other local groups to host informal community events on evenings and weekends. Unfortunately, when the landlord removed the tables and chairs, myself and others didn't have any recourse. The landlord did not communicate clear plans for the Underground and was not willing to work with community members to support public space in Crystal City. Small businesses in the Underground were also forced to close around the same time.

Empty hallway in The Underground

Even before tables and chairs were all removed from the Landing, the quality of the space was affected by top down develoment initiatives. One example was that some of the tables and chairs started were removed, and the tables became damaged. The landlord refused to replace them. I also contacted the landlord offering to help pay costs for replacement tables and chairs, and I was ignored.

The Landing after tables and chairs were removed

When I lived in Crystal City, The Landing was a great place to meet people. I become friends with two people who lived in my apartment building because we met at The Landing. I also enjoyed being able to easily walk there. The Landing also encouraged a culture of self organizing and collective ownership, which was reflected in how events were run. When the host wasn't able to be there for game nights, other regulars including myself would make sure things ran smoothly. This helped me when I become the official host.

In 2016, Arlington County decided to open a popup library in the Crystal City Underground called The Connection with close input from neighborhood residents. It was originally proposed to be open for 9 months. However, it stayed open until 2019, and avocacy from locals played a key role in this happening. This included the Crystal City Civic Association(CCCA) making a formal request to the county, and individual requests to the Arlington County Board. CCCA president Carol Fuller said "The Library has become a focal point for Crystal City". I lived in Crystal City when the Connection was opened, and I enjoyed having a library very close to where I lived. Even after I moved away from Crytal City, I would still occassionally visit the Connection.

Unfortunately, the Connection Closed in December 2019 because Arlington decided that paying for Library staffing was not a priority. A restaurant and makerspace nearby closed, which decreased foot traffic. The landlord did not prioritize finding alternative tenants to draw foot traffic, or provide a space in a more visible location for rent. The lanldlord was the dominant real estate developer in Crystal City due to its ownership of the Underground and other properties in Crystal City. This left very little room to negotiate. Arlington also did not have the money available to construct a new library.

In 2022, the landlord agreed to build a new library as part of another development. In March 2024, Arlington asked for $5.8 million from the landlord as compensation for not building a library, and there is no other landlord in Crystal City with available space for a library.

An example of the limitations of top-down development without community input came from the recent rennovation of an outdoor plaza with food vendors. The space was empty when I went there on a Friday evening around 7pm, and I noticed that some stalls were empty. The lack of climate control or covering from rain limits its usability as a public food court. An empty space without obstacles such as chairs and tables would have been far more usable as a park and would also be cheaper.

Crystal City outdoor food plaza. The tall building in the background is luxury apartment building constructed by the same landlord. As of 3-4-2026, the lowest monthly rent for a 1 bedroom was $3,070.

Community spaces make people more invested in the quality of the neighborhoods, which makes government spending on the neighborhoods more effective. Bottom up action doesn't happen in a vaccum by isolated individuals. Local investment is also far more stable than depending on large investments from outside entities. Community spaces will also help people work together and form local businesses, which create jobs and keep more wealth in the area.
