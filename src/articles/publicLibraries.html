The value of public libraries as community spaces in DC

{{>authors/articleHeading name='Bharat Ponnaluri' date='April 2026' imageLink='https://createthirdplaces.nyc3.cdn.digitaloceanspaces.com/people/Bharat_Picture.jpg' }}

I am a resident of DC living in Dupont Circle who hosts community board game events and advocates for more public spaces. DC Public Library(DCPL) has done a great job of running libraries and developing them into public spaces for the benefit of the community. It is critical that DCPL has the necessary funding to continue their success.

In addition to providing physical books, libraries promote social connection and reduce isolation that is a major health risk. According to a 2023 analysis done by former Surgeon General of the United States Vivek Murthy, social disconnection causes an increased mortality risk equivalent to smoking up to 15 cigarettes.

Over the past 10 years, finding space to host events in DC has become increasingly difficult as the availability of community spaces declined. Other hosts I've talked to have had challenges. I was part of one group that moved it's regular location 4 times within the span of a year.

Back in 2016, many of the community gathering spaces were in bars and restaurants that are no longer options. Many places have closed, reduced their hours, or raised food prices. While many new restaurants have opened, the ones with space typically have high food prices.

Picture of the south DuPont Circle Metro. The vacant former Panera location is on the right. (Photo by Bharat Ponnaluri)

With the decline in public spaces, libraries have become increasingly important. They are free to use, provide space for community groups, and provide learning opportunities.

However, hosting events in libraries isn't always feasible due to limitations on hours or space availability. This is a sign that library space should be increased to meet interest.

Why libraries are great

As accessible community spaces, libraries have the following benefits.

  • Local neighborhood improvements are best done by residents working together. More community spaces through libraries provide opportunities for neighbors to connect with each other, and form partnerships to work on local improvements.
  • Supporting access to libraries gives people the opportunity to make friends at library events. In DC, travelling to a library likely involves walking or taking public transit. This allows them to get a better view of their community and connect with other locals.
  • Libraries provide learning opportunities. With the rapid changes we are seeing in our economy right now, it is important for adults to have opportunities to learn new things that will help them adapt to changes. For kids, libraries are a quiet place to study and receive academic help outside of school, and also join community groups for people their age.
    • Programs for young kids at Shepherd Park Neighborhood Library. (Photo by Bharat Ponnaluri)
  • Reading physical books is correlated with improved cognitive skills and ability to focus among all ages, and we should be supporting access to them. Books don't have distractions that cause people to get sidetracked, and people have to mentally engage with book content in order to understand it. Community groups at libraries also help people improve their attention span through face-to-face conversations and offline group activities.

Recommendations

Here are the areas where I think DCPL has been the most successful. Continued support is essential to support the continued success of DCPL.

1. Support community groups using libraries

Fliers for community groups in Takoma Park Neighborhood Library. (Photo by Bharat Ponnaluri)

2. Support adult learning services

Community board at library with learning resources for adults. (Photo by Bharat Ponnaluri)

3. Use libraries as a way to demonstrate how what great community spaces look like

DC should increase library funding to maintain current services and give employee salaries a cost-of-living adjustment.

DCPL has successfully avoided staff cuts, even with budget challenges, and staff cuts should be avoided in the future. Staff are essential to running successful libraries. They should be compensated well and be able to rely on a sense of stability with their jobs. This includes giving raises to match cost of living increases. According to the Bureau of Labor Statistics, consumer prices rose 3.0% for the 12 months ending in March, including a 16.5% rise in energy costs and a 4.5% rise in food costs.

According to an April 2026 analysis I did of salary data from the DC Department of Human Resources, the median monthly compensation for a DCPL employee was $5,743. Meanwhile, the average monthly rent for a 1 bedroom apartment according to Apartments.com was $2,250.

Avoiding staff cuts also means minimizing the time cost with hiring when the budget situation improves. Also giving a sense of stability means staff aren't distracted by looking for other jobs as a risk mitigation measure.

In 2025, DC's proposed budget was going cut DCPL library hours by a third in Fiscal Year(FY) 2026. Fortunately, Councilman Matthew Frumin found $6.5 million to maintain library hours. Instead of trying to avoid future cuts, we should be figuring out how to increase library hours

Budget limitations and the need to hire additional staff limit the ability to increase hours right now. However, increasing hours with help from residents should be a long term goal. Additional hours will give more people opportunities to use the library, especially community groups that meet late in the evening or on weekends.

Details about library worker salary analysis

Salary information for DCPL employees is public information and can be seen here. I did a search on DCPL employees with the browser network tab open, and a server reponse was returned with employee data. I saved the response to my computer and then used a Python script to get the median salary. 

import math
import statistics as stat

import json

def main():
    with open('data/employees.json','r') as file:
        data = json.load(file)
    
    employeeData = data["data"]

    salaries = []
    for employee in employeeData:
        salaries.append(employee['comprate'])
    
    print(stat.mean(salaries))
    print(stat.median(salaries))

if __name__ == '__main__':
	main()
