Why urban areas should focus on incremental density increases over building high rises

{{>authors/articleHeading name='Bharat Ponnaluri' date='April 18, 2026' imageLink='https://createthirdplaces.nyc3.cdn.digitaloceanspaces.com/people/Bharat_Picture.jpg' }}

Urban areas often have underdeveloped property in high density areas. This can be seen in Arlington, Virginia. Incremental density increases on these properties would help with tax revenue and reduce housing costs.

Starbucks in Rosslyn near high rise. (Photo by Bharat Ponnaluri)

Density should be gradually increased while minimizing construction of high rises that are more than 10 stories tall. Lower costs of smaller density increases are more accessible to residents who want to participate in neighborhood development.

Building under construction between rowhouses in DC (Photo by Bharat Ponnaluri)

Overall, Arlington has done a great job of densifying through transit oriented development(TOD) around Metro stations. However, local challenges have prevented Arlington from fully implementing its TOD vision.

Picture of Clarendon facing east. By Wikipedian1234 - Own work, CC0, Link

Challenges caused by high rise construction and underdevelopment

Constrained housing availability combined with Arlington's desirability have caused steady cost increases that are making the county unaffordable. According to Redfin, the median sale price of a home in Arlington in March 2026 was $815,000. A RentCafe.com analysis from March 2026 found that the average monthly rent of a 1 bedroom apartment in Arlington was $2441. High rise costs are contributing to the rents. For every additional floor, the construction cost per floor increases due to requirements including increased building support needs and elevators.

Arlington has become increasingly dependent on property taxes and a significant portion of that comes from high rises.56% of tax revenue came from property taxes in fiscal year(FY) 2026.

Surface parking lot in Arlington. (Photo by Bharat Ponnaluri)

Costs can cause high-rise developments to be paused after approval. It's difficult to pivot or encourage small incremental developments as alternatives. For example, there is one unsued plot of land in Arlington that is worth around $180 million according to 2026 assessment data.

$180 million plot of in Arlington land surrounded by density. (Photo by Bharat Ponnaluri)

Benefits of incremental density increases on underdeveloped space

Promoting incremental density improvements encourages smaller local developers to get involved. Construction costs for high rises are a barrier for these developers. These density improvements can also be done by retrofitting existing buildings without constructing additional space.

Small condo building on DC street/ with rowhouses (Photo by Bharat Ponnaluri)

Smaller density improvements are also more effective at promoting community spaces that are essential to a thriving neighborhood. Lower costs will mean lower rents. Smaller local developers are more likely to have a connection to the neighborhood where they are building, which makes them more likely to be creating improvements that locals want.

Connection Library in 2017. (Source: Wikimedia Commons)

Also, development done by smaller local developers means competition that incentivizes improving neighborhoods for residents. On the other hand, a monopoly makes it easier to neglect amenities such as public gathering places and a library.

Hallway in Crystal City with vacant space. The glass doors on the right used to be entrances to the Connection Library. (Photo by Bharat Ponnaluri)

Recommendations

1. Remove minimum parking requirements

Arlington has minimum parking requirements for developments near Metro stations. Removing parking requirements allows density increases with fewer floors, which reduces costs for smaller developers.

2. Implement higher land value taxes

Taxes on land should be gradually increased, while property taxes should be lowered. In Arlington, developers are incentivized to sit on underdeveloped or vacant property. Potential rent earnings are a factor used to determine property values. High value vacant land can become mortgage collateral or an investment tool. These uses cause price volatility that can affect property tax revenue. Meanwhile, property taxes represent additional development costs.

These incentives are demonstrated by a plot of land in Arlington owned by JBG Smith that has been empty since 2020. The land value of increased from $20 million in 2024 to $50 million in 2025. JBG Smith is facing financial difficulties from declining revenue and expenses. Their 2025 financial report filed with the SEC shows more than $1.5 billion in debt obligations from 2026 to 2028, which is more than 3 times their current revenue.

JBG Smith's $50 million empty plot of land.(Photo by Bharat Ponnaluri)

If JBG Smith defaults on debt, they would lose property used as collateral. That could cause instability which is a risk to Arlington's property tax revenue.

3. Modify development rules to help small developers

Building code requirements such as egress rules, HVAC design constraints, and fire codes are complicated. The complexity of the requirements should be reduced to help small developers while making sure safety isn't compromised. It is often cost prohibitive for small developers to build properties in a way that meets the requirements. Meanwhile, larger developers have experience working with the requirements and can afford to take extra time to meet them.

Small developers also have a harder time getting financing because banks often see them as unknown risks. They can also develop tiny pieces of land that are unsuitable for large development projects favored by big developers. They are also more likely to use and develop the property they own because they don't have income from other properties.

4. Support public spaces

Arlington has an Adopt-a-Park program, that helps residents work with staff to improve and maintain parks. Involvement with public spaces helps people get to know other locals whom they can work with on incremental developments.

Park run by community group in Arlington (Photo by Bharat Ponnaluri)

Empty spaces can also become into pop-ups. They are great for experimenting with ideas and making spaces useful while waiting for development.

Pop-up park in Arlington. (Photo by Bharat Ponnaluri)

5. Walk around

Seeing locations physically nearby gives ideas and shows things that are not working. For example, I found out that Arlington has an Adopt-a-Park program when encountering a park run by a local community group.
