Why urban areas should focus on incremental density increases over building high rises

{{>authors/bharatPonnaluriHeading}}

Urban areas often have plots of underdeveloped or vacant property in high density areas. This can be seen in Arlington, Virginia. Overall, Arlington has done a great job of increasing density in urban areas through transit oriented development around Metro stations. However, Arlington has room for improvement.

This can be developed to help the local government with tax revenue, and give more people an opportunity to enjoy the benefits of living in Arlington. Encouraging incremental density improvements also empoweres locals.

Density should be incrementally increased in urban areas, while minimizing high-rises that are more than 10 stories tall. Density should also be concentrated in certain areas, such as by following a transit-oriented development approach to prioritize density around public transit stops.

Challenges caused by high rise construction and underdeveloped land

High rise construction and undeveloped land can caues issues for revenue stability, even in places like Arlington county that have done a relatively good job of increasing density. In Arlington, 56% of tax revenue came from property taxes in fiscal year(FY) 2026. The proposed FY 2027 budget represents a small decrease from the previous year, and one cause is slow revnue growth

In January 2026, property values increased by only 1.1%, representing the third consecutive year of declining growth. The costs of many services rose further.

High rises can also become expensive. For every additional floor that is build, the cost per floor increases. Construction on lower floors will need to support more weight from higher floors, which means more building supports. Also, elevator space requirements per floor go up. Elevators will have to travel to more floors, increasing wait time, and more floors means more people that will be using elevators. On the other hand, when building in urban areas, it is more cost effective to create buildings that are more than 1 story high, because the price of land is high.

These financial limitations can cause developments to be put on hold after plans for high rises have been approved, and it is difficult to change what is being developed.

This can be seen by empty plots of land in urban areas in Arlington, including the site of the former Americana hotel owned by DC area real estate developer JBG Smith. JBG Smith is currently facing financial difficulties reflected in declining revenue and losses. Their 2025 financial report filled with the SEC shows more than $1.5 billion in debt obligations over the next 3 years, which is also more than 3 times their current revenue.

If JBG Smith tries to sell property, that will put downward pressure on real estate prices. If JBG Smith defaults on loans, they will lose property that is put up on collateral, which will cause instability. Both scenarios are a risk to Arlingotn County, which relies on property taxes.

Why high rises are built

Despite the cost ineffiicencies of building high rises, they are still built. Understanding why this happens is important when determining how we should promote incremental density increases

Higher floors, are considered desirable, which can be seen by rents being higher for apartments on uppoer floors. For example, in April 2026, a 1260 square foot 5th floor apartment 3 bedroom square foot apartment in the Grace apartment building in Arlington cost $4,810 in monthly rent. On the other hand, a smaller 3 bedroom apartment on the 23rd floor was $5,355 per month in rent.

Taller buildings also attract attention, which is great marketing for the developers who build them. They also generate interest from people visiting, which is evident in Manhattan. A 2021 report that showed that most of New York City's tourism jobs were in Manhattan.

The process of planning a development to get the necessary approvals takes time, which is an incentive to build one tall building, instead of multiple smaller buildings, which requires going through the planning proccess multiple times.

Larger properties also encourage development of nearby land While this doesn't translate to every single piece of land surrounding the large property, there is still an effect on property values. The original developer will see a significant increase in property values from proximity to new developments. Empty plots of land also become desirable, because their value will go up as new developments surrounding them are created.

If a developer isn't facing financial difficulties and has large amounts of money, then the risk of building an expensive high rise is likely to be seen as low. The sale price of the building is based on the construction cost, which provides an opportunity to recover the costs paid for construction, assuming property values are not disrupted. Meanwhile, there are opportunities to make money off rent, and rising land values from other developers creating buildings nearby

Benefits of incremental density increases on empty land

Promoting incremental improvements allows smaller local developers to get involved. Incremental density increases also mean that low density land isn't going to be turned into high rises, where the cost per square foot makes it unafforable for small develoeprs.

Incremental improvements are also beneficial for promoting community space. The lower cost of low to mid rise density with incremental density changes, means that developers can charge lower rents to be earning money from the building. This makes it easier for locals without the large financial resources of businesses to rent the space. Smaller local develoeprs are more likely to have a connection to the place where they are building, which makes them more likely to be building developments that locals want.

Also, having development done by smaller local developers also means that the developers are competing with each other. A dissatified resident can move to another location in the same neighorhood where another developer will be running their property in a different way. On the other hand, if one developer monopolizes a neighorood, a renter unhappy with where they live doesn't have options if they want to stay in the same neighorhood. This means they can more easily neglect neighborhood ammenities that people want such as public gathering places or a library.

Implement a program to allow residents to participate in developing and maintaining public spaces. Arlington has a program called Adopt-a-Park, that supports locals in adopting outdoor spaces, and helping to maintain and improve the space. Locals are also encourged to work with park staff, which helps them get more involved.

Having residents involved in developing and maintaining public spaces helps them get to know other locals and get first hand experience with developing public space, which will help them make other helpful suggestions. This can also be a great way to create temporary parks while waiting for redevelopment to start.
