Why urban areas should focus on incremental density increases over building high rises

{{>authors/bharatPonnaluriHeading}}

Urban areas often have plots of underdeveloped or vacant property in high density areas. This can be seen in Arlington, Virginia. Overall, Arlington has done a great job of increasing density in urban areas through transit oriented development(TOD) around Metro stations. However, local challenges have prevented Arlington from fully implementing its TOD vision.

Arlington has empty and underdeveloped land near Metro stations that should be incrementally developed to increase density. This would help with tax revenue and give more people the opportunity to enjoy the county's benefits. Encouraging incremental density improvements also empowers locals to participate in determining how a neighborhood is developed.

To minimize construction costs and support incremental development, density should also be increased without building high rises that are more than 10 stories tall.

Challenges caused by high rise construction and underdeveloped land

Rents in Arlington County have steadily increased. This is a sign that Arlington is a desirable place to live. However, the prices are making Arlington increasingly unaffordable. According to RentCafe.com analysis from March 2026, the average monthly rent of a 1 bedroom apartment in Arlington was $2441. The average for Virginia is $1864, the average for Washington, DC is $2303. Constrained availability of apartments due to underdeveloped land is contributing to the cost.

The cost of the high rise apartments constructed in Arlington is also contributing to unaffordable rents. For every additional floor that is build, the cost per floor increases. Construction on lower floors will need to support more weight from higher floors, which means more building supports. Also, elevator space requirements per floor go up. Elevators will have to travel to more floors, and more floors means more people that will be using elevators.

This pattern of development is also causing issues with revenue stability. In Arlington, 56% of tax revenue came from property taxes in fiscal year(FY) 2026. The proposed FY 2027 budget represents a small decrease from the previous year, and one cause is slow revenue growth. In January 2026, property values increased by only 1.1%, representing the third consecutive year of declining growth. The costs of many services rose further.

On the other hand, Arlington has 1 story commercial buildings near Metro stations on expensive land where incremental density increases should be done.

Arlington also has surface parking lots near Metro stations.

Surface parking lot in Clarendon. (Photo by Bharat Ponnaluri)

High rise construction also is faster than incremental development over time. This gives private developers a level of control that limits the ability of local governments or individuals to have influence. This can be seen by a 1989 Arlington County planning document that mentioned rapid development of Crystal City as a concern. Most of Crystal City's development in the previous 30 years was done by one real estate company. The legacy of this can be seen by the level of control JBG Smith currently has over Crystal City. This allowed JBG Smith to back out of a 2022 commitment to build a library in Crystal City by paying $5.8 million, despite objections from residents.

The financial limitations can cause developments to be put on hold after plans for high rises have been approved, and it is difficult to change what is being developed. One example of this can be seen by the stalled PenPlace development for Amazon HQ2. The land for the PenPlace development is unused and is worth around $180 million according to 2026 assessment data.

Empty PenPlace development. (Photo by Bharat Ponnaluri)

Why high rises are built

It is important to understand why high rises are built, in spite of their cost inefficiencies. The understanding is important to determine how we should promote incremental density increases.

Higher floor are considered desirable, which can be seen by their rents. An example of this can be The Grace Apartments in Crystal City. For example, in April 2026, the monthly rent on a 663 square foot 1 bedroom apartment on the 7th floor was $2,495. The rent identical apartment directly above on the 17th floor was $2,930. The higher rents are also evidence of the costs of building high rises.

The process of planning a development to get the necessary approvals takes time, which is an incentive to build one tall building. Multiple smaller building or improvments require going through the planning process multiple times.

Larger properties also encourage development of nearby land. While this doesn't translate to every single piece of land surrounding the large property, there is still an effect on property values. The original developer will see a significant increase in property values from proximity to new developments. Empty plots of land also become desirable, because their value will go up as new developments surrounding them are created.

Benefits of incremental density increases on empty land

Promoting incremental improvements allows smaller local developers to get involved. Incremental density increases also mean that low density land isn't going to be turned into high rises, where building costs per square foot are unaffordable for small developers.

Incremental improvements promoting community space. The lower costs of small density increases or renovating existing space, reduce the necessasity of charging high rents. As a result, rent will probably be lower. Smaller local developers are more likely to have a connection to the place where they are building, which makes them more likely to be building improvements that locals want. This also means less burdensome rent increases, since they are more likely to care about resident's financial situations.

Connection Library in 2017. (Source: Wikimedia Commons)

Also, having development done by smaller local developers means competition that incentivizes improving neighborhoods for residents. A dissatisfied resident can move to another location in the same neighborhood where another developer will be running their property in a different way. On the other hand, if one large developer has a monopoly, a renter unhappy with the developer doesn't have optionsif they want to stay in the same neighborhood. This means they can more easily neglect amenities that people want such as public gathering places or a library, and force unecessary rent increases.

Hallway in Crystal City with vacant space. The glass doors on the right used to be entrances to the Connection Library. (Photo by Bharat Ponnaluri)

Recommendations

1. Remove minimum parking requirements

Arlington County currently has minimum parking requirements for development, even if most of the parking is unnecessary because of proximity to a Metro station. Removing parking requirements allows density increases with fewer floors, which reduces costs. As a result, development will be more accessible to small developers.

Removing parking requirements would not mean parking spaces would completely go away, and developers can still decide to add parking.

2. Modify other development rules to help small local developers

The complexity of building requirements should be reduced to help small developers. Currently, the complexity is a significant limitation for small developers due to limited experience and a lack of resources to spend on understanding the complexity.

Small developers also have a harder time getting financing because banks tend to see them as unknown risks.

Small developers can also develop tiny pieces of land that are unsuitable for large development projects favored by big developers. They are also more likely to use and develop the property they own. They are far less likely to be owning large amounts of highly valuable property elsewhere to earn income, and probably won't have millions of dollars for investment.

3. Implement higher land value taxes.

Taxes on land should be gradually increased, while property taxes should be lowered. In Arlington, developers are incentivized to sit on underdeveloped or vacant property. Allowing community use of property or renting property at a discounted rate could lower the property value. In Arlington, potential rent earnings are a factor used to determine property values.

Empty land with a high value can be used as collateral for mortgages. The land can also be used for speculative investment. These can contribute to price volatility, which makes it harder for local governments to get reliable revenue from property taxes. Meanwhile, property taxes represent an additional cost for development.

This can be seen by an empty plot of land in Crystal City owned by JBG Smith that has been unused since 2020. The value of the land increased from $20 million in 2024 to $50 million in 2025. JBG Smith is currently facing financial difficulties reflected in declining revenue and losses. Their 2025 financial report filled with the SEC shows more than $1.5 billion in debt obligations over the next 3 years, which is also more than 3 times their current revenue.

JBG Smith's $50 million empty plot of land.(Photo by Bharat Ponnaluri)

If JBG Smith defaults on loans, they will lose property that is put up on collateral, which will cause instability in the land value. This is a risk to Arlington County, which relies on property taxes.

Public space

Residents should be encouraged to participate in developing and maintaining public spaces. Arlington County has a program called Adopt-a-Park, that supports residents who want to adopting outdoor spaces. Through this program, they work with county staff to maintain and improve a park.

Having residents involved with public spaces helps them get to know other locals. They also get first hand experience with developing public space, which will help them advocate for other spaces. They also bring locals together, which helps people make friends. This can also be a great way to create temporary parks while waiting for redevelopment to start.

Walking

Seeing locations physically close by will give ideas and also show things that are not working. For example, I found out that Arlington County has an adopt-a-park program when walking past a park run by a local community group. Repeatedly walking around an area can also help you get a more accurate understanding of how things are changing over time.
