Advice on Hosting Events in Third Places

{{>authors/articleHeading name='Bharat Ponnaluri' date='May 15, 2026' imageLink='https://createthirdplaces.nyc3.cdn.digitaloceanspaces.com/people/Bharat_Picture.jpg' }}

This page contains advice on hosting public events in third places. While this advice is based on my experience running board game events, much of it can be applied to other activities.

1. Have recurring events at the same time of week and place

Regular events are easier to remember, and people are morely likely to make them a part of their schedule. As host, regularity will also reduce the need to advertise events because people are more likely to know when they are happening. Depending on availability, having events once a week, every other week, or some other predictable interval is good.

Recurring events will also make it easier for regulars to help you advertise events.

Make sure attendees know what to expect and don't make the event too open ended

I used to advertise events as generic gaming events with a focus on a variety of 1-3 hour modern strategy games. I wanted to play a variety of games, and I accommodate a wide variety of preferences. However, events did not go the way I had hoped.

The variety of games being played caused challenges with teaching and learning games. People become tired of constantly learning new games instead of playing familiar games, and then stopped showing up to events. I also noticed other regulars become increasingly hesitant to teach games. The overwhelming majority of games I played that were longer than 1 hour involved someone teaching the rules.

I also had a difficult time teaching games. I often couldn't get help from other people at the table since they were all unfamiliar with the game. During games, I had a hard time trying to take my turns and answering questions when trying to teach 2 or more new players. The variety of preferences also added challenges to teaching. Game were often played once because someone was not interested in playing it again.

The variety of preferences also made it difficult to select games that everyone wanted to play. During my weekend afternoon events, we would often have difficulty agreeing on a game due to different preferences for length and complexity. The best case scenario was often playing a medium length and complexity game that people tolerated. However, the game wasn't one of someones' preferences. In other instances, we would have to pick a game that one or more people disliked. These people would then have to pick between the option of playing a game they weren't going to enjoy, sitting out, or going home. I noticed that they also stopped showing up to future events.

My weekend afternoon events were often very unpredictable due to the variety of preferences. This made it hard for me to convince others to attend because I couldn't describe a predictable experience that they would enjoy. It also became harder for me to bring games that others would likely enjoy due to uncertainty about who would show up and their interests.

Having event focused on a narrower set of high interaction games has helped my weekend afternoon events. Teaching games is significantly easier because there are often other people at the table who know how to play and can help me. It also has resolved issues with incompatible preferences and people not wanting to show up because of uncertainty about what types of games will be played.

Example of an open ended event description for an event I hosted.

Let's play board games @ The Artemis in Washington DC(3605 14th St NW, Washington, DC 20010).

We have a wide variety of games that we are happy to teach. Feel free to bring your own games as well. Here are some examples of the games we like to play.

  • Concordia
  • Castles of Burgundy
  • Puerto Rico
  • Werewords
  • Resistance: Avalon

If you are taking the Metro, the Artemis is located near the Columbia Heights Metro station. There is also plenty of street parking nearby.

 Event page

This description gives off the impression that I am hosting a generic gaming event. The board games have a wide range of complexity and average play time, from less than 15 minutes to more than 2 hours. Also, many people were not familiar with all of the games listed.

A better description would be something similar to the following.

We have a wide variety of games with a focus on resource management games that take between 1 and 3 hours to play. Also, we will play some social games. We are happy to teach games.

Here are some examples of the games we like to play.
  • Concordia
  • Castles of Burgundy
  • Puerto Rico
  • Werewords
  • Resistance: Avalon

Most of the regulars of the group I was hosting the event with preferred medium length resource management games with some shorter games afterwards. I think the revised description would have been more aligned with the games the regulars wanted to play. If you end up trying to cater to a wide variety of preferences, often nobody will be happy. Disagreements over games will mean people don't get to play what they enjoy.

Also, one should be careful about accomodating requests from other people about events, when those people have never attended an event. For example, shortly after I became a regular event host in 2021, a reporter from a local news channel asked to come and film an event. After a brief conversation, they told me they wanted to film people playing shorter more social games, while my event was focused on longer games.

I ended up turning down the request to film an event because I thought it would be disruptive to the people there to play board games. Also, I didn't see a reason to have the events get attention on a news broacast, since many viewers would not be interested in an event to play resource management games. My events already had challenges with people not being able to agree on games, and I didn't want to add further difficulties.

Get other people involved

Consistently successful events require involvement from others, not just the host. As a host, you are only 1 person, which limits what you can do, especially if you are busy at times outside the event. Other people can also help identity issues that you may have missed, which is very helpful when your event is larger than a group of people that can sit at 1 table. Involvement from regulars also helps them advocate for their preferences.

I noticed this firsthand when I started showing up to Friday board game nights at the Landing in Crystal City in 2014. There was a collective sense of ownership over the event, with many of the regulars involved in teaching games and welcoming people who were there for the first time. As a result, event ran smoothly for years, even when an official host was not able to show up.

Having other people actively involved in running events has been helpful for my weekend afternoon events. For example, if I'm introducing a game to people who haven't played before, someone else who is familiar with the game is likely to be playing. They help me by guiding new players who are looking for clarification on how to play, especially when I'm focused on my current turn. Regulars have also helped me with hosting events when I was not able to show up.

Encourage good behavior and minimize personality conflicts

In my experience, behavioral problems can be a significant challenge for events. I consider good behavior to be anything that is not disruptive or inappropriate.

First of all, I prefer to avoid calling out someone unless they are doing something that is disruptive or inappropriate. I think it's important to be accepting of personality and communication differences, and saying these are wrong would be a matter of option. I don't want people calling out behavior based on their opinion or encourage norms of people criticizing others for minor things.

If someone is doing something very disruptive or inappropriate, it's important to deal with that behavior quickly, especially if it's part of a pattern. I'll directly tell the person that their behavior is not acceptable and take further action if necessary. Banning someone should also be on the table as on option, however, a ban should only be done in extreme cases were there is a serious instance of negative behavior or ongoing problems that aren't being resolved.

During events, I prioritize trying to lead by example to show good behavior. I find that people are far less likely to be disruptive and inappropriate when others are showing that behavior is not acceptable. In case of minor instances of someone being inappropriate or disruptive, I find that trying to start a conversation about an unrelated topic is helpful. This helps defuse any conflict situations that may be arising and moves the person away from the negative behavior. Usually, I do not directly call out the behavior. Sometimes, the behavior is a minor one-off thing that can be ignored. A lack of responses can show the person that their behavior should not be repeated.

For example, there was one instance during an event where someone was insistent on playing the card game Uno although everyone else clearly wanted to play something else. I started a conversation with the person about game groups and recommended a group where people were likely to be interested in playing Uno.

There was another instance where someone was insistent on playing this particular resource management game called Dune: Imperium although others were not interested. I showed them some other board game groups where they could play that game.

I also start my weekend afternoon events with a quick social game. I find that this is a good opportunity for people to get to know each other, which helps minimize personality conflicts. Also, I find that the social nature of these games is helpful for showing people that disruptive or inappropriate behavior will not be tolerated.

Encouraging other people to get involved in helping to run is also very helpful here.
