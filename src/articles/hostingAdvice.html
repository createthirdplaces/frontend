Advice on Hosting Events in Third Places

By Bharat Ponnaluri

This page contains advice on hosting public events in third places. While this advice is based on my experience running board game events, much of it can be applied to other activities.

Have recurring events at the same time of week and place

Having regular events will make it easier for people to remember your events and make the events a regular part of their schedule. As an organizer, regularity will also reduce the amount of time needed to spend on advertising an event because people are more likely to know it is happening. Depending on availability, having events once a week, every other week, or some other predictable interval is good.

Recurring events will also make it easier for regulars to help you advertise events.

Make sure attendees know what to expect and don't make the event too open ended

When hosting public board game events, I used to advertise the events as generic gaming events with a focus on a variety of 1-3 hour modern strategy games and shorter games. I was interested in playing a variety of games, and I wanted to accommodate a wide variety of preferences. However, events did not go the way I had hoped.

Due to the wide variety of games being played, there were challenges related to teaching and learning games. People become tired of constantly learning new games instead of playing games they were familiar with, and stopped showing up. I also noticed other regulars become increasingly hesitant to teach games. The overwhelming majority of games I played that were longer than 1 hour involved someone teaching the rules.

I also found teaching games to be challenging. Since I was often teaching games that other people were unfamiliar with, I didn't have other people helping me. This became a significant challenge when teaching 2 or more new players because I had a hard time concentrating on my moves and answering questions. The variety of preferences also added challenges to teaching. Often, a game wasn't played more than once because someone was not interested in playing it again.

The variety of preferences also made it difficult to select games that everyone wanted to play. During my weekend afternoon events, we would often have difficulty agreeing on a game due to different preferences for length and complexity. The best case scenario was often playing a medium length and complexity game that people tolerated. However, the game wasn't one of someones' preferences. In other instances, we would have to pick a game that one or more people disliked. These people would then have to pick between the option of playing a game they weren't going to enjoy, sitting out, or going home. I noticed that they also stopped showing up to future events.

My weekend afternoon events were often very unpredictable due to the variety of preferences. This made it hard for me to convince others to attend because I couldn't describe a predictable experience that they would enjoy. It also became harder for me to bring games that others would likely enjoy due to uncertainty about who would show up and their interests.

Having event focused on a narrower set of high interaction games has helped my weekend afternoon events. Teaching games is significantly easier because there are often other people at the table who know how to play and can help me. It also has resolved issues with incompatible preferences and people not wanting to show up because of uncertainty about what types of games will be played.

Example of an open ended event description for an event I hosted

Let's play board games @ The Artemis in Washington DC(3605 14th St NW, Washington, DC 20010).

We have a wide variety of games that we are happy to teach. Feel free to bring your own games as well. Here are some examples of the games we like to play.

  • Concordia
  • Castles of Burgundy
  • Puerto Rico
  • Werewords
  • Resistance: Avalon

If you are taking the Metro, the Artemis is located near the Columbia Heights Metro station. There is also plenty of street parking nearby.

 Event page

This description gives off the impression that I am hosting a generic gaming event. The board games have a wide range of complexity and average play time, from less than 15 minutes to more than 2 hours. Also, many people are not familiar with the games listed, which furthers the impression that I was hosting a generic gaming event.

A better description would be something similar to the following

We have a wide variety of games with a focus on resource management games that take between 1 and 3 hours to play. We are happy to teach games.

Here are some examples of the games we like to play
  • Concordia
  • Castles of Burgundy
  • Puerto Rico
  • Werewords
  • Resistance: Avalon

Most of the regulars of the group I was hosting the event with preferred playing medium complexity resource management games, and focusing on their preferences would be good. If you end up trying to cater to a wide variety of preferences, often nobody will end up happy because disagreements over games means people don't get to play what they enjoy.

Get other people involved

Consistently successful events require involvement from others, not just the host. As a host, you are only 1 person, which limits what you can do, especially if you are busy at times outside the event. Other people can also help identity issues that you may have missed, which is very helpful when your event is larger than a group of people that can sit at 1 table.

I noticed this firsthand when I initially started showing up to Friday board game nights at the Landing in Crystal City. There was a collective sense of ownership over the event, with many of the regulars involved in teaching games and welcoming people who were there for the first time. As a result, event ran smoothly for years, even when an official host was not able to show up.

Having other people actively involved in running events has been helpful for my weekend afternoon events. For example, if I'm introducing a game to people who haven't played before, someone else who is familiar with the game is likely to be playing. They help me by guiding new players who are looking for clarification on how to play, especially when I'm focused on my current turn. Regulars have also helped me with hosting events when I was not able to show up.

Encourage good behavior and minimize personality conflicts

In my experience, problems with other peoples' behavior can be a significant challenge for events. I consider good behavior to be anything that is not disruptive or inappropriate.

First of all, I will avoid calling out someone unless they are doing something that is disruptive or inappropriate. I think it's important to be accepting of personality and communication differences, and saying these are wrong would be a matter of option. I don't want people calling out behavior based on their opinion or encourage norms of people criticizing others for minor things.

If someone is doing something that is very disruptive or inappropriate, it is important to deal with that behavior quickly, especially if it is part of a pattern. I'll directly tell the person that their behavior is not acceptable and take further action if necessary. Banning someone should also be on the table as on option, however, a ban should only be done in extreme cases were there is a serious instance of bad behavior or ongoing problems that aren't being resolved.

During events, I prioritize trying to lead by example to show good behavior. I find that people are far less likely to be disruptive and inappropriate when others are showing that behavior is not acceptable. In case of minor instances of someone being inappropriate or disruptive, I find that trying to start a conversation about an unrelated topic is helpful.

Sometimes, I may try to quietly start a conversation with a person who is not behaving appropriately. This helps defuse any conflict situations that may be arising and moves the person away from the negative behavior. Usually, I do not directly call out the behavior. Depending on the situation, I may suggest another alternative action as well.

For example, there was one instance during an event where someone was insistent on playing the card game Uno although everyone else clearly wanted to play something else. I started a conversation with the person about game groups and recommended a group where people were likely to be interested in playing Uno.

There was another instance where someone was insistent on playing this particular action selection game. I showed them some other board game groups where they could play that game.

I also start my weekend afternoon events with a quick social game. I find that this is a good opportunity for people to get to know each other, which helps minimize personality conflicts. Also, I find that the social nature of these games is helpful for showing people that disruptive or inappropriate behavior will not be tolerated. Usually, they find this out, when myself and the others ignore the behavior. Otherwise, a quick comment is usually enough.

Encouraging other people to get involved in helping to run is also very helpful here.
