Bars, Malls, and Restaurants as Third Places

{{>authors/articleHeading name='Bharat Ponnaluri' date='May 2026' imageLink='https://createthirdplaces.nyc3.cdn.digitaloceanspaces.com/people/Bharat_Picture.jpg' }}

Bars, malls, and restaurants are widely used as public spaces. However, they aren't always third places designed for people to linger and interact. Here are criteria that should be considered when evaluating a bar, mall, or restaurant as a third place.

  • Ability of local residents to walk there.
  • Space availability including table space and opening hours.
  • Ability to stay for a long period of time.
  • Ability for people to regularly use the space. This includes affordable food prices, accessibility, and being welcoming to anyone who wants to use the space.
  • Support for interaction with other people.

    People should buy food by talking to a human without a self-ordering kiosk or using a phone. This allows the people running the place to get feedback from visitors and make improvements. Computers cannot have a conversation with a human. The place should also make it easy for people to have conversations with each other.

Running a public space can be expensive due to costs including rent payments and food. As a result, it is often hard for the owner of a public space to maintain it as a third place. We should be encouraging local governments to enact policies to support public spaces functioning as third places. This page has more suggestions

An ideal third place

One example of a third place was the Landing in Arlington, Virginia, which is shown in the image at the top of this page. It was open 24/7 with plenty of available table space, and people could stay there as long as they wanted. There was a wide variety of low cost food options nearby, and people could also chose to not purchase anything. Also, people who lived nearby could easily walk there.

An example of a good third place

Tryst in the Adams Morgan neighborhood of DC is an example of a good third place, although it isn't ideal. Tryst is located in a walkable neighborhood with decent availability of table space and some low cost food. People can also linger and have conversations with others.

However, the space can get crowded. Some of the food options are slightly on the expensive side and cost around $15. Tryst also supports delivery and carryout through online ordering, which I don't consider as a good thing for a third place. While I thought the employees at Tryst were doing a good job when I went there, support for delivery and carryout suggests that some of the owners' focus is away from running Tryst as a third place. Orders can also be placed online without the human interaction from an in person order.

I went to Tryst at around 8:30pm on a Tuesday near the end of a trivia night. The place was mostly full aside from a handful of small tables. The trivia host did a great job and I found them to be engaging. There was music there that was somewhat loud, although I could hear background conversations.

After trivia ended, the place became quieter because the music stopped. People started to leave after trivia ended. I could see people standing outside and talking. The sidewalk outside of Tryst is large, and there is some outdoor seating. I'll often stand outside and talk with people after events in a third place, even if the place is open. Being outdoors is a refreshing change of scenery.

I also was someone walking in the door around 9:30pm, and one of the employees gave them two free cups of ice water. It was relatively warm, and I could feel it as I was walking home. The water was a nice gesture for someone who did not need to make a purchase.

Some people were working on laptops for a while, which I see as a sign that it is ok for people to stay for a long period of time. I also noticed many other people there sitting at tables who appeared to be finished with their food, which suggests that people were not rushed out.

An example of a bar that was missing some of the qualities of a third place

The Board Room in Arlington, Virginia was a popular public gathering space until it closed in April 2026.

Reasons the Board Room was a good third place

  • Walkability: It was easy for residents living at nearby homes to walk nearby. There were also apartments built on floors above the Board Room.
  • Food was ordered by speaking to a bartender or server.
  • When space was available, people could stay until the Board Room closed. In 2026 before it closed, the Board Room was open until at least midnight every day.

Reasons the Board Room was not a good third place

  • Food prices: Food at the Board Room became very expensive. In April 2026, a typical meal cost well over $10, with a limited selection of cheaper options.
  • Inconsistent table space availability: Sometimes, table space was not available due to crowds or reservations for events. There was also table space in side rooms that people could not use when I went there on Wednesday evenings.
  • It was someones difficult to have conversations due to loud music or background noise from activities the Board Room was running.
