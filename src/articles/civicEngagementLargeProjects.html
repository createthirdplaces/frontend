Why civic engagement is important to the success of large development projects

{{>authors/articleHeading name='Bharat Ponnaluri' date='April 2026' imageLink='https://createthirdplaces.nyc3.cdn.digitaloceanspaces.com/people/Bharat_Picture.jpg' }}

Large development projects guided by civic engagement are important to a thriving urban area.

One example, can be seen by the success of Arlington County's transit-oriented development(TOD) policy, which has been shaped by local input. The TOD policy transformed the Rosslyn-Ballston corridor into a thriving urban area and was a key part of ensuring the success of Metro's Orange Line.

Civic engagement and development of the Rosslyn-Ballston corridor

Rosslyn-Ballston corridor. (Photo by Arlington County)

At the start of the 1950s, Arlington had a zoning ordinance that restricted development of most land to single-family developments with lot size requirements of 5000 square feet or more. In 1957, Arlington identified revenue challenges caused the fact that people living in single-family homes contributed 48% of county revenue while requiring 68% of expenditures. To avoid tax hikes, Arlington rezoned neighborhoods to permit density increases, including ones along the Rosslyn-Ballston corridor.

Local advocacy and Arlington's TOD success

The route to TOD success started in the 1960s during planning for the Metro. Initially, the Orange Line of the Metro was planned to be above ground in Arlington in a highway median. However, the county decided to pursue TOD and worked with local residents to change plans so that the Orange Line would be underground between Rosslyn and Ballston. Later, county planners worked with residents to develop a 'bull's eye' plan for density around the 5 Metro stations on the corridor.

Taking residents' input into consideration with TOD would be a key to its success. In a 1968 referendum, 78% of Arlington voters supported a $54 million bond issue to support the DC Metro. Arlington has also been careful to avoid upzoning areas near Metro stations with single family homeowners without their consent. This shows a consideration for residents, which is essential for a thriving urban area, and it has prevented significant opposition to TOD from homeowners.

Arlington County continued pursuing a TOD policy that has turned the Rosslyn-Ballston corridor into a resounding success. According to a 1970 census, Arlington had around 30,000 detached single-family homes, and that number remained steady over the following 50 years. Meanwhile, the stock of other types of housing increased from around 41,000 units to 88,000 units, and Arlington's population increased by around 60,000. There have also been additional density increases due to TOD in Crystal City, Pentagon City, and the Columbia Pike corridor.

TOD also shows that density can be increased without more traffic. A study done in Arlington from 1996 to 2012 showed traffic decreases along many roads. The steepest observed decline of 23% was from Wilson Boulevard, which is the main street running through the Rosslyn-Ballston corridor.

How Ballston shows that TOD promotes urban resiliency

Arlington's TOD policy was a key part of Ballston's resiliency during the COVID-19 pandemic. Many people were avoiding the Metro and restaurants along the Rosslyn-Ballston corridor had to rely on takeout from people living nearby. Due to the number of people living in Ballston, local restaurants did well.

Many apartments in Ballston are mixed-use developments with restaurants on the ground floor. I lived in one of these developments, and enjoyed having a convenient food option nearby.

Ballston apartment building with ground floor restaurant. I lived on the third floor. (Photo by Bharat Ponnaluri)

Suggestions

Here are some suggestions for promoting civic engagement that will support large projects while also supporting smaller projects.

1. Encourage collaboration between local government agencies and residents to develop public space

In 2016, Arlington opened the Connection library pop-up in the Crystal City Underground after working with residents to develop the plans. Although the library was only planned to be open for 9 months, it remained open until the end of 2019, and support from Crystal City residents played a key role. The library was also a great example of adaptive reuse for a space in that would have otherwise remained unused. It was also near the Crystal City Metro station and helped Arlington's TOD policy.

Connection Library in 2017. (Source: Wikimedia Commons)

Pop-ups are also great for experimenting with ideas and making spaces useful while waiting for development.

Pop-up park in Rosslyn. (Photo by Bharat Ponnaluri)

Recently, Arlington started a program for people to share library memories. This included a place at the Central Library where people could write down library memories to post on a wall.

Central Library Share a Favorite Library Memory. (Photo by Bharat Ponnaluri)

Another successful example can be seen by Arlington County's Adopt-a-Park program.

Park run by community group in Courthouse.(Photo by Bharat Ponnaluri)

2. Support outdoor plazas

Outdoor plazas are a great place for local residents to gather and promote civic engagement. They help people connect and find ways to work together to support their neighborhoods.

Ballston plaza with restaurant seating. (Photo by Bharat Ponnaluri)

Plazas are also a great opportunity for restaurants to expand their space with some outdoor seating.

The Ellipse in Ballston. (Photo by Bharat Ponnaluri)

3. Support pop-up stores from small businesses

A pop-up store is a great starting point for a local business due to lower upfront investments and not having to pay rent for a regular storefront.

Pop-up art store in Ballston. (Photo by Bharat Ponnaluri)

4. Concentrate density increases in specific areas

It is easier to support areas of concentrated density with public transit. The higher capacity of public transit is important to make sure people can move around more quickly in large urban areas. Community amenities such as libraries and parks near large clusters of density can serve more people.

Long Bridge Park in Arlington (Photo by Arlington County Parks & Recreation Department)

Density increases also should not be limited to areas with frequent public transit service. In these cases, density should be concentrated around a shopping and dining street or a landmark such as a public park. One result will be making additional public transit more viable.
