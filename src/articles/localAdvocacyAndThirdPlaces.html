Ancient Rome and other historical examples

One reason the Roman Empire was successful is the fact that relative many other ancient states, it was good at being inclusive. The Roman Empire was still a highly unequal society built on exploitation of human labor and plunder. However, the tradition of civic engagement for a long time that reduced the impact of these problems.

Later, the Roman Empire had issues where wealth started being consolidated in the hands of rich people, while slavery continued. Attempts to earn money through plunder also continued. People started caring less about Rome’s success because they didn’t see much to gain from Rome’s rule. During the 5th century AD, the Western Roman Empire fell. As the Western Roman Empire lost territory before the fall, former Romans living in those territories often did not care about trying to restore Roman rule.

A similar pattern was seen with the Byzantine Empire which lost control over most of Anatolia in the 11th and 12th centuries. Turks took over and many residents did not care or welcomed Turkish rule. During this time, Byzantine leaders were busy trying to prioritize personal power over supporting an inclusive civil society that people wanted to be a part of. During this time, the Byzantine Empire allow a massacre of Latin residents from Italy to take place, which ended up contributing to the long term instability and fall of Constantinople.

Ancient Sparta was another example where the lack of inclusive civic engagement led to it's downfall. Sparta was the dominant power for some time in Ancient Greece, but it was run by a small elite while most of the population known as helots lived as slaves.

Relations with other cultures.

When Rome made some efforts to encourage good relations with other culture, Rome was able to get along with them and treat them with relative decency. On the other hand, Rome's mistreatment of places like Carthage and Corinth, encouraged continued acts of unjustified violence against both cities. Eventually, the violence contributed to Rome deciding to burn both of those cities.

Successful countries across history are ones where people are invested in the success without being overly nationalistic. A country cannot be successful if nobody cares about helping it do well. On the other hand, excessive nationalism is also bad. It directs the country to be aggressive towards neighbors and target people living in the country for arbitrary reasons. It also makes people focus on arbitrary things that are seen as being in service of the nationalism. It doesn’t matter how good a country may be doing on paper in terms of measures such as wealth and territory, trying to harm others is not a measure of success.

Rome is a partial historical example of this due to how Roman people’s attitudes towards helping had an effect. While the Romans were known for nationalism gone too far such as destroying Carthage when the city wasn’t threatening Rome, and mass enslavement, Roman history still shows how civic engagement had a positive effect in other areas.

For example, Rome was often very successful, even after suffering from major defeats such as Cannae during the Second Punic War. The key was Romans cared about the success of their country. Apathy towards maintaining the Roman empire was a major factor in causing the empire to decline. Yes, there were a lot of external problems such as the Huns invading. However, these problems were exacerbated by ineffective Roman responses. Many Romans were preoccupied with other things or they were busy fighting each other in destructive civil wars.

The benefits of local advocacy

Local advocacy has significant benefits for neighborhoods in terms of quality of life and health. It also makes local economies more sustainable. On the other hand, a lack of local advocacy can cause a neighborhood to be vulnerable to real estate bubbles that have long term effects.

Technological development and pro social values

Pro social values become increasingly important as tech becomes more powerful. As technology gets more powerful, its ability to cause destruction increases. However, it’s ability to benefit society increases. Giving up technology would be irresponsible as it would be a way of making our lives worse for no reason. It is also impractical as we have become used to living with technology. As a result, the best way to deal with new technology is to promote more prosocial values.

If we fail to develop more pro social values, the ability of technology to cause destruction will become an existential risk. I’m also concerned about tech reducing pro social values because it makes it harder f or people to deal with social issues.

I think AI can be a threat to society, and it’s not because of super intelligent killer robots. AI can promote anti-social values by replacing human interaction such as with chatbots, so they are less concerned about being constructive with technology. It can also be used to cause people to develop a psychological dependence on technology, which prevents them from trying to deal with issues such as climate change.

Establishing a tradition of local advocacy

Local advocacy is not created overnight. Establishing a tradition of local advocacy takes time, and it is important to make sure that process is not disrupted.

The long term effects of disrupting civic engagement

For example, in Detroit, it is estimated that highway construction and "urban renewal" displaced almost 100,000 residents in the 1950s/60s. Meanwhile, existing neighborhoods were divided. Black residents were disproportionately affected, and were prevented from moving to suburban neighborhoods due to racial covenants. This was in stark contrast to Arlington County, which was actively supporting civil rights at the same time. Detroit started showing visible signs of decline. In her 1961 book, The Death and Life of Great American Cities, Jane Jacobs said: "Virtually all of urban Detroit is as weak on vitality and diversity as the Bronx .... It is dispirited and dull and almost deserted by seven o'clock of an evening". Jacobs was referring to a place having a variety of uses. However, these signs were largely ignored. Detroit's economy entered a decades long decline leading to the city losing half its population, and bankruptcy in 2013. As the decline was ongoing, the lack of civic engagement made it difficult to develop a sustainable long term solution. In the Bronx, large numbers of residents were displaced for the purposes of "urban renewal" including construction of the Cross Bronx Expressway starting in 1948 that displaced between 40,000 and 60,000 policies. The pattern of disruption continued through the 60s and 70s and started to include widespread fires. These were often deliberate acts of arson by landlords, and the New York City government deliberately took away municipal services, especially firefighting. Property values in the Bronx were also deliberately kept low through urban renewal and racist policies of redlining. Meanwhile, black people were blocked from moving into newly constructed neighborhoods nearby. Eventually, it is estimated that 80% of housing in the Bronx was destroyed and 250,000 people were displaced.

Establishing a tradition of civic engagement in Arlington County

Arlington's history of civic engagement started before the 1900s through civic associations. Later, continued examples of civic engagement would be seen in the 1940s, 50s, and 60s. A group of citizens formed the "Committee of 100" to allow people to discuss issues. This was seen as a way to discuss all sides of an issue prior to making a decision to make sure there was consensus and to deal with any issues with the potential decision. In 1949, the Arlington school board became the first in Virginia to be democratically elected instead of appointed due to advocacy from a local citizen's group.

Arlington was also welcoming to new residents. Arlington experienced rapid growth in the 40s and 50s and many new residents actively become involved in local government. Ellen Bozman who moved to Arlington County started an extended care program for children of working parents in the 1960s. In 1973, Bozman was elected to the Arlington County board, and remained on the board for 24 years.

Elizabeth Weihe was another prominent community member. Weihe become involved in advocating for school related issues after moving to Arlington in 1947, become involved in efforts to desegregate schools, and was appointed to the county planning commission in 1960. When Weihe was a member of the Planning Commission, she got a Metro car in front of a department store to generate support for a funding referendum. People could walk in the car to get an impression of what it would feel like and also see others.

Arlington County's success in promoting civic engagement can be seen by early support for desegregation. The success shows that people were willing to challenge rules that did not make sense and a desire to be welcoming towards people. Arlington was the first county in Virginia to integrate schools, and this went against Virginia state government rules that mandated separation between black and white people.

During this time, Arlington demonstrated an ability to anticipate and handle future challenges. In the 1960s, County Board Member Tom Richards saw that development would threaten public space availability. He decided to save land for parks and make sure it was connected. Later, he would also work to support the Metro for providing transportation. Arlington would also promote a transit-oriented development(TOD) policy that would focus development around Metro stations, which allowed for continued preservation of parkland.

Arlington's tradition of civic engagement would also be key to the long term success of the Metro. Initial plans for Metro's Orange line were to have it run in a highway median. County planners worked with residents to push for the Orange Line to run underground between Rosslyn and Ballston, where it became a catalyst for development through TOD.

Local advocacy and development

On the other hand, less successful local advocacy can also lead to stagnant development in neighborhoods. One example can be seen by the Cleveland Park neighborhood in Washington, DC where there is a surface parking lot and strip mall outside the underground Metro station. This is a place that can be converted to housing or public amenities that improve the quality of life for more residents. When walking by the parking lot in the evenings, I notice that it is almost empty.

The Cleveland Park neighborhood is very walkable, so residents don't need a car to access developments there. People can also travel to the neighborhood by biking, the Metro, or a bus. However, Cleveland Park did not have the level of community buy-in that Arlington had for development around the Metro, and there are no plans to develop the surface parking lot that does not serve local residents.

To develop this area and similar ones, close collaboration with local residents is necessary. Resilient and successful neighborhoods require local involvement, and forcing development will be disruptive. Its also important to respect the views of local residents, when they have different thoughts on how a neighborhood should develop.

Suburbs of DC have had a mixed degree of success when it comes to TOD with some Metro stations having unused land near entrances. They are mostly organized as counties, with many of people not living near a Metro station.

On the other hand, the cities of Alexandria and Rockville have been successful with implementing TOD around Metro stations. These cities are significantly smaller than the nearby suburban counties with people who are more likely to live near a Metro station. Both cities were also founded before the 1900s, and were walkable my necessity. The age of these cities also means that local advocacy had more time to develop.

People living further away from TOD locations are less likely to see noticeable benefits from the development. People who are far away from transit they won't use will be concerned about being forced to subsidize TOD infrastructure through their taxes. Concerns by suburban residents about subsidizing TOD infrastructure should be addressed by making sure the costs are being covered by the people and businesses directly benefiting. This will also help establish trust that tax money is being spent wisely. On the other hand, the counties that have been less successful with TOD around Metro stations were also relatively sparsely populated in 1900.

When trust has been established, residents will be more likely to support TOD with upfront taxes for the long term economic benefits.

How a lack of local advocacy can contribute to economic bubbles and instability

Cities need to be continually developing in order to accomodate current and future residents. However, distorted incentives promote too many coarse-grained developments. These developments are large buildings that do not meet the needs of residents.

Coarse-grained developments lock out smaller developers who have limited cash flow and borrowing power. They also raise the cost of land, which increases property taxes for residents and makes housing more expensive. Also, they contribute to long term economic instability. Addressing the distortions will ensure that new building construction benefits current and future residents while improving economic stability.

Coarse-grained developments magnify economic distortions and create a self-reinforcing cycle that encourages more coarse grained development. Residents will likely have to pay more for housing leaving them with less spending money. The costs may also push out some residents and prevent people from moving in.

Meanwhile, coarse-grained development can lead to ignoring community amenities because they are not suitable as investments. For example, in the Crystal City neighborhood of Arlington, Virginia, the local civic association asked for a new library. However, the one developer, JBG Smith that owned suitable space for a library successfully cancelled a commitment to Arlington to build one. JBG Smith also has more than 100,000 square feet of empty space in Crystal City. If a large developer creates public amenities, they are likely to become tools to draw investment that will start being ignored when they are no longer useful to increase property values.

JBG Smith has been prioritizing coarse-grained development and mentions affordability as a business risk. Their 2025 annual report filed with the SEC says: "Increased affordability of residential homes and other competition for tenants of our multifamily properties could affect our ability to retain current residents of our multifamily properties, attract new ones or increase or maintain rents, which could adversely affect our results of operations and our financial condition".

Coarse-grained developments will often remain empty for years. Developers are also often incentivized to make it harder for small businesses and residents to use the space. One way they can do this is by requiring tenants to rent out a large space that is beyond the means of most people and businesses.

An example can be seen a high rise in the Rosslyn neighborhood of Arlington, Virginia called 1812 North Moore. It was constructed to speculate on the economic recovery from the 2007-2009 Great Recession. After construction finished in 2013, the building remained vacant until 2017 when Nestlé moved its US headquarters there. Meanwhile, Rosslyn's office vacancy rate tripled from around 10% to more than 31% between 2011 and 2014. In 2015, the office vacancy rate across Arlington was at a historic high of 21%. However, as of the end of 2015, the developer did not decrease the rents. The developer's CEO also mentioned that floor-by-floor deals for smaller businesses would affect the value of other nearby buildings.

Coarse grained developments can also reinforce inefficient land use. One example is a big box store with a large surface parking lot. It is expensive for local governments to maintain infrastructure for big-box stores. Also, if a retail tenant leaves, the space is likely to remain vacant due to the cost of redevelopment.

For example, there is a shopping center called the Potomac Yard Center in Alexandria that is close to half a mile long and contains multiple big-box stores. When I went to the parking lot on a late April 2026 weekday shortly before 5pm, there were many empty spaces. One of them was also being used as a storage space for a snowplow.

Developers might also demolish a large building to prepare for redevelopment. Afterwards, they can sit on a property if redevelopment stops. One example is an empty site in Maryland near the North Bethesda Metro station which used to be White Flint Mall. The developer closed the mall in 2015 and later demolished it as part of a redevelopment plan.

After White Flint Mall was demolished, the redevelopment stopped. As of April 2026, the site of the mall was empty. White Flint Mall had flaws and was overdue for redevelopment. However, a mall is a better use of land compared to empty space.

Distortions from unnecessary large building construction can cause a real estate bubble to form. When the bubble pops, jobs and small businesses will be affected, and local governments will face lower tax revenues. Meanwhile, the neighborhood will not be able rely on help from residents who were pushed out due to high prices. Successful and resilient neighborhoods rely on residents who are familiar with the area and know other residents they can work with. People are also likely to move out if they lose their job and need a cheaper place to live.

The pop of a bubble can cause lasting problems. The decline of property values will cause problems for local governments which rely on property taxes to fund essential services. A negative spiral could start as a deterioration of essential services cause further property value decreases. Meanwhile, poverty rates will increase as jobs disappear. In Detroit, a decades long decline led to the city losing more than half its population, and bankruptcy.

The collapse of a property bubble in 2007 demonstrates the risk of distorted investments. The bubble collapse triggered a global financial crisis, and a major recession known as the Great Recession.

During the Great Recession, urban housing costs continued to increase. According to data from the US Bureau of Labor Statistics, housing costs in urban areas went up by 3.38% during the recession, while inflation was only 1.58%. In the New York City area, the cost of housing went up by 3.93%, despite being the epicenter of the global financial crisis.

Using software as a tool

Software is helpful for advocacy. However, it should be used as a tool in specific areas where it makes sense. Software should not be seen as the default solution for a problem. This section will include a significant amount of high level advice aside from specific areas. People work differently, and technology that is useful for one person may not be useful for other.

First of all, when using technology, you should be aware of why you are using it. Otherwise, technology can end up using a lot of your time. There have been many instances where I decide I'm going to quickly check something online, and end up browsing for more than an hour.

Internet usage can also have negative effects besides wasting time. Information overload from browsing a lot of content can be a distraction. There is also content that is misleading, incorrect, or designed to trigger negative emotions.

One useful rule of thumb is to make sure the app or website you are using has very clear boundaries on functionality, like looking at email, or going to a weather forecast website to see if will rain in the evening.

I don't always make websites using HTML and CSS. My personal website is using a Wordpress site I set up more than a decade ago. While there are limitations on the functionality, the Wordpress setup is functional for the blog posts and static webpages on the site.

In general, I do think the overwhelming majority of websites should be created using some sort of website builder tool such as Wordpress. Website builder tools have been around for years, and do not require programming experience. For example, if you are hosting a website for a fast casual restaurant, the main goal of the website should be showing information including the location, menu, and opening hours. Ordering food can be supported through an 3rd party online ordering tool or a phone number that can be called to place an order.

When picking a website tool, you should stick with some sort of deterministic tool. LLMs can create AI generated websites for informational content that are functional. However, their output can be random and unpredictable.

Also, using LLMs to create websites goes against the fundamental principle that AI should only be used for tasks that have objectively measurable success outcomes. Websites are viewed by humans, and human preferences can't be consolidated to numerical metrics that an LLM can understand. LLMs also lack the ability to gather details about real life context that affects how a website can be viewed. As an example, in the board game groups I regularly attend, many people dislike the game Catan or are not very familiar with it. However, Catan is one of the most popular board games, and many other people enjoy playing it including myself.

Also, be careful about chasing technology hype by focusing on new technology just because someone says it is good. It takes time for people to determine if a technology is useful and where it is worth using. Also, new technologies are likely to be in an early state of development and may change significantly in the upcoming future.

For example, there was a time when blockchain technology was seen as the next big thing. Cryptocurrencies and NFTs were viewed as the main applications of blockchains. After people learned more about blockchain and experimented with use cases, people lost interest in the technology and stopped seeing it as a game changer.

The hype behind NFTs was arguably worse than the hype around cryptocurrencies, as NFTs were even less useful. The idea behind an NFT was that it was a piece of digital art that used blockchain technology to track ownership. However, showing an NFT meant that someone could just take a screenshot and have a copy for themselves.

Prioritizing UX.

It is a well-known fact that UX should be prioritized. However, the reality is that UX is often ignored. It is important to understand why in order to address the issue. I think the issue is related to misaligned incentives and a lack of understanding of issues. UX issues seem to be significantly affecting revenue, even when companies are profitable for other reasons.

Often, I noticed a customer or internal stakeholder at work will ask for a new feature. Other people will get on board to seem lie they are supporting something useful. ON the other hand customers are far less likely to suggest UX improvements and might just chose to stop using a product with poor UX. An internal stakeholder is less likely to see UX issues as a problem, since they aren’t actively using the product to solve a problem.

It is far harder to sell improved UX as something that useful because a new feature is more noticeable to upper management. As a result, stakeholders are incentivized to focus on ignoring UX in order to look good to upper management. Another issue is the proliferation of one size fits all software and tools such as Tailwind CSS and GitHub Copilot that do not account for different UX preferences. Also, the frameworks are not capable of talking to customers and finding out what users are looking for.

Different people have different perspectives on what good UX is. For example, Arabic is read from right to left, so someone who knows Arabic and not English is going to have a different idea of what they want a user interface to be compared to someone who can only read English.

Developing a website to help people meet each other

One example of useful software is a website to help people meet in person. The idea would be for the website to show people information about in-person events that they would then attend. There would also be no support for messaging or posting public comments. If people want to talk online, they should meet in person and chose to share contact information.

Websites where people can promote events in third places tend to prioritize keeping people online. Meetup.com was a notable exception until quality dropped and prices increased. Competitors including Luma and Partiful follow the same venture capitalist funding model initially used by Meetup, and they are on the path to enshittification. This includes one former competitor Eventbrite, which has been purchased by the company Bending Spoons. Bending Spoons owns Meetup.com and is responsible for its latest problems.

The website would be part of a decentralized platform. Event information would be shared through an open API and aggregator websites could display information from multiple event websites.

One example would a website for chess events in DC and another website for resource management board games such as Catan. An aggregator website could then read information from the chess website and Catan website, and allow users to search for events related to both games. The board game aggregator website could then have its data read by a DC area aggregator website that combines information from other types of websites such as a DC sports event website.

While this architecture is decentralized, it might be desirable for an organization to have an implementation of this under centralized control. One example would be a local parks agency managing events for multiple parks. In this case, decentralization would come from the fact that managers of individual parks can have event sites, while sharing the events with an aggregator that can be used to search for events across multiple parks.

A similar architecture has seen success in settings where centralized control outside of the computer systems is necessary. For example The Create Third Places decentralized architecture is similar to the Bell-LaPadula security model with discretionary access control which was used by the US Department of Defense.

To summarize, the Create Third Places architecture and any other decentralized architecture has an effective decentralization level based on the centralization level of the outside entities controlling the individual parts. Create Third Places, blockchain, or any other decentralized architecture is not capable of forcing decentralization on a system that an outside entity wants to centralize.

Decentralization is good for multiple reasons. It is difficult for a general purpose website to meet the interests of specific hobbies or communities. People have a variety of different interests that will be complex to effectively represent on one website. Also, it is impossible for the developers of one site to have knowledge about every single community and hobby. While a centralized solution can be more efficient when it comes to technologies, there are rapidly diminishing returns when it comes to improving user experience for every use case.

Smaller websites can have content related to the interests of their audience without affecting how others see content they are interested in. As an example, dmvboardgames.com, a website promoting in person board games in the DC area has a page for local board game designers.

Local community organizers can submit event information to a large aggregator website without being dependent on them. If the large website does something similar to Meetup.com and starts charging $300 per year to host groups while downgrading their UI, the owners can move to a different aggregator or not use one.

It is easier for individual communities to move to an alternative with a decentralized network. If the quality of an aggregator website declines, users can switch to an alternative aggregator site without data that is on a group website. Group websites owners can also remove access to an aggregator if they think that aggregator should no longer be used. On the other hand, it is very difficult to move off a centralized solution that has declined in quality. There is also no process in place to vote out a centralized solution that has declined in quality such as Meetup.com.

A decentralized network will facilitate the creation of high quality localized group websites. People working on local group websites are more likely to personally know their users. This will help them connect with the users and get a better understanding of what they are looking for.

Having independent local websites also gives opportunities for more people interested in software development to develop skills for working with small organizations and individuals. As a result, more people will be able to make a living from software development while keeping more wealth in their local communities. Even with the challenges of catering to the interests of a variety of people, a general purpose Meetup alternative would be more efficient as fewer people will be required to develop and maintain it. However, this efficiency comes with the costs of fewer people getting learning opportunities or participating in the development of community building websites.

The impossibility of developing and maintaining AGI is one reason why I’m not allowing the use of LLM generated code. Sustainable AGI is impossible, even with infinite computing power. Communication challenges will also prevent AGI from being achieved. A successful AGI system is going to be complex and require large teams of people working together and sharing knowledge. There are plenty of historical examples where one group of people that are part of the same country ends up splitting up when that country loses territory. This leads to cultural divergence. We can also notice a similar process occurring over a shorter period of time. The size of ancient empires was limited by communication challenges, and a similar principle also applies to large organizations. Positivity

The organization should avoid making unnecessary negative statements about people, groups of people or other organizations.

If the negativity is towards a specific issue that isn’t related to a person, too much negativity is still bad.

The group might be benefiting society in some way and may be an essential part of people’s lives. While people will complain about games they don’t like, successful board game events aren’t focused on complaints. At a successful board game event, people are enthusiastic and positive about games they want to play.

Attitudes towards Werewolf are another example of how positivity is a good thing. In Werewolf, the idea behind the game is that players are part of a village, and some people are secretly Werewolves. The Werewolves are trying to eliminate the villages, and the villagers are trying to eliminate the Werewolves. Players can try to eliminate Werewolves during the day phase. To do this, a player nominates someone for elimination, and if they get votes from more than half the players in the game, they are eliminated unless another player got more votes.

Some players will have roles that give some information. However, the information is limited, and many players will be villagers with limited information. As a result, executions are often random, and people dislike this. A second issue is that players can be eliminated early in the game, and Werewolf games can often last for more than an hour.

While I don’t dislike Werewolf, I prefer a game with less randomness and player elimination. However, I rarely talk about my issues with Werewolf. Instead, I will try and convince people to play other hidden identity games. My favorite is Resistance: Avalon. In the game, players are going on quests to find the holy grail, and there are hidden minions of Mordred who are trying to sabotage the quests. Players aren’t eliminated in the game, and there is more information. Each round, players are selected for a quest. If you are not trusted, you are unlikely to go on quests. However, you can still participate in the game through voting on quests and talking. Also, the minions of Mordred have an alternative win condition, which is to identify the player who is Merlin. Merlin has information about the minions of Mordred, and is trying to help win quests without being obvious.

Another example of positivity here is related to the game Blood on the Clocktower. Blood on the Clocktower is similar to Werewolf. There are two significant changes that address issues with Werewolf. The first is that eliminated players are still able to talk and they have one remaining vote for the rest of the game, so they aren’t sitting there watching people play. The second is that every player in the game has a special role, and the roles give significantly more information. For example, there is a role called the undertaker that learns the role of a player that was eliminated during the previous day. As a result, voting on eliminations is less random.

I’ve seen this positivity through interactions with people and groups playing Clocktower. They are very enthusiastic about Clocktower, and want more people to play. They aren’t complaining about how much they dislike Werewolf. One example is with this local Clocktower group that I’ve played games with. They started with a small group of people enough for one group at one event per week. They have expanded to host multiple events per week, and I’ve seen close to 60 people and 3 games running at some events. People in the group were very friendly and welcoming and prioritized inclusivity.

Positivity should not mean ignoring preferences. For example, I was at a board game event where someone proposed playing a Norse themed auction game called Nidavellir. I played the game once and I was not in the mood to play it again. An example of taking positivity too far would be faking enthusiasm for the game and then saying I wanted to play Nidavellir.

Being too negative by saying no, and complaining about things I didn’t like was also not a good idea. It would not have helped us find an alternative, and it would’ve given people a wrong impression. Nidavellir is still a good game despite my lack of interest in playing it. One person who owned the game enjoyed it and was looking for others to play the game with. The other people I was trying to make a game decision with could’ve ended up enjoying the game if they tried playing it at a future event, and they should form their own impression of the game without being biased by my lack of interest in playing it. Instead, I decided to suggest some alternatives for what to play.

In addition to making people feel comfortable and have a good experience, I find that being welcoming to others is a great way of encouraging positive behavior. In board game groups, I have noticed some instances of problematic behavior from people which include harassment, bullying, and trying to dominate conversations to prevent certain people from talking. People have a variety of reasons for engaging in this behavior.

While people who engage in problematic behavior are a minority, they have a significant negative effect on a public gaming group. I believe that one important way of reducing instances of problematic behavior is leading by example and treating people with kindness in respect. This sends a signal that problematic behavior is unacceptable, and I’ve noticed that it makes people less likely to engage in problematic behavior. I see running a group and ensuring acceptable behavior as a shared responsibility and not something that should be handled by only one person.

People engaging in problematic behavior when leading by example will receive hints or direct comments specifically about the unacceptable behavior. It is also far easier for a person to make a meaningful change when they have some positive behavior they can use as an example. Also, when it is necessary for someone to stop engaging in some behavior because it is problematic, giving an example of positive behavior as an alternative is often better.

For a public group, listing instances of problematic behavior to avoid is difficult and not always effective. It can also make things worse by encouraging too much focus on paying attention to people who might be doing something wrong, which can lead to toxic nitpicking of behavior based on opinions. It also doesn’t help people understand how they should be engaging in positive behavior.

Even with leading by example, I’ve noticed that it is sometimes necessary to directly confront someone and tell them to stop doing something because they will ignore acceptable norms of behavior. In this case, being able to point to other examples of people treating others with kindness and respect is useful. Also, this helps ensure that multiple people point out that a behavior is unacceptable, which helps make sure it stops.

If people are too exclusive in public groups, this makes it harder to lead by example as a way of reducing how often people engage in unacceptable behavior. If people are excluding others, this sends a signal to others that it is ok to be rude and mistreat others, and these behaviors can escalate into behavior that is worse. Also, if people are being exclusive, that means that they are not paying attention to everyone in a group, which means that they are less likely to notice problematic behavior.

However, it is important to make sure being welcoming doesn’t get taken too far. It does not mean every event should be public, despite the fact that Create Third Places focuses on public events. Sometimes people want to have events that are exclusive for a variety of reasons including being interested in a specific activity, wanting to interact with specific people they know, or wanting an event with a limited number of people. I believe exclusivity crosses a line when an exclusive group pretends to be welcoming, acts exclusive at an event that is supposed to be for people to meet others, or they are engaging in negative behavior to prevent others from joining.

For example, if one walks into a board game cafe, groups of people who are already there should have to be responsible for including the person who walked in. On the other hand, if the cafe was hosting a board game meetup, existing groups of people should make an effort to welcome someone who walked in. This doesn’t necessarily mean playing a game together or having an extended conversation because there might be significant differences in game preferences or personalities.

Also, I don’t see wanting to play games with people who have specific interests as exclusivity. For example, if I want to run an event to play Settlers of Catan, I’m not going to include someone who only wants to play Monopoly. I’ve also noticed that people who take exclusivity in public groups too far often also show other instances of problematic behavior. By preventing exclusivity from going to far, these people will not show up unless they are making an effort to behave in a positive manner.

One challenge I’ve seen in DC is the fact that many indoor public spaces in fast casual restaurants and cafes close and remain empty for years. Many of the spaces that are available also tend to be somewhat expensive. This seems to be a challenge that is widespread across the US. I was reminded of this as I passed by a closed coworking space called Cove in DC. I could see inside and I was thinking about how it would be converted into a public space.

Too much focus on commenting about the decline of public spaces is of limited effectiveness because it doesn’t help come up with a solution for how these closed locations can become public spaces. By showing how great public spaces can be created and by promoting public events, this will give opportunities for people to figure out solutions for improving the supply of accessible public spaces.

Other people and organizations will also be working to promote community building. They should be seen as partners trying to accomplish the same goal. This also means engaging with people who are interested in community building and helping them find ways to get involved. This also means encouraging them to consider working with other organizations if that will help the goal of community building.

The ability for people to work positively with coworkers is important. If I’m choosing between hiring two employees, B whom would be a less productive employee with a strong ability to work with others and has a positive attitude, and A who would be a more productive employee who doesn’t work well with others and would be rude, I would chose to hire B.

This is even true if I knew that A would do a better job of helping the organization meet its goals. Other employees are likely to take extra time and effort to deal with the challenges of working with A, and that means they have less free time. If they don’t take the extra time, that will lead to extra stress.

The risk of decreased efficiency with using AI

If a company has success with a tool, jobs will be cut, which can lead people to start useless projects that cause inefficiency. Also, the effectiveness of AI tools is inconsistent, and the productivity drain from tools being ineffective needs to be factored in. There will also be a wide disparity in the quality of AI tools and their adoption rates.

As a result, some groups will see rapid short term productivity gains relative to others. They will become highly desirable, so getting in will become highly competitive. This will breed an overly competitive environment that causes long term problems.

People will deliberately start useless projects or ones that make a product worse if it means boosting their reputation among managers. Often managers want useless products or features that make the user experience worse because of money or because the update is seen as a good thing by influential people. An efficient AI is also going to lead to job cuts, so people will come up with useless projects in order to justify their jobs.

A team that is successful using AI to increase productivity will see a rapid spike in revenue and prestige, and there will be external and internal pressure to continue that prestige. However, the prestige gain might be based on a successful product or update that can’t be replicated with something else. As a result, they will start coming up with useless ideas that maintain the prestige and profit, even if they cause long term problems or degrade product quality. These useless projects will become entrenched and cause increasing amounts of inefficiency.

Why language intelligibility explains challenges with tool churn and increasing complexity

There are a variety of languages that are spoken around the world. People who speak two different languages can communicate by both learning a third common language, learning each others’ language, or communicating with the help of a translator. They work because of predictability. Once a person is fluent in a language, a person will typically not forget to speak a language. Languages also change slowly, which helps people maintain the ability to speak and understand the language. The consistent patterns make it easier to learn because learning materials are more consistent.

Programming tools tend to change somewhat quickly relative to languages. Imagine if someone who was fluent in Spanish 10 years ago would not be fluent now, or they would be fluent in another language you didn't know. Things would devolve into chaos. Lately, organizations have been creating their own tools. They include concepts specific to the tool that do not apply to other tools. Examples include AWS, Tailwind CSS, and React. Because organizations are creating so many tools with their own specific guidelines for how things should be done, becoming an expert in everything is impossible. This is even true when focusing on tools in one area. For example, AWS, Google Cloud Platform and Azure are 3 major cloud providers and there are many smaller ones.

Systems will become harder and harder to maintain as they are designed around increasingly complex stacks of products that most people do not know. This increasing complexity is a natural consequence of the fact that these systems are already complex. When a system is complex, people have a hard time figuring out the ideal way of making a change. As a result, they end up making a change in a way that adds complexity.

Guidelines for different tools are not logically consistent with each other, which increases onboarding challenge, and makes increasing tech debt more likely. During 2025, tech companies saw record profits and stock prices. These are being driven by monopolies and clever marketing. As a result, they aren’t incentivized to spend money on building better products. Eventually, the complexity of their systems will become unmaintainable to the point where they can't be modified to manipulate users and generate ad revenue. As a result, profits will collapse and they will return to a more sustainable profit level or fail. As a result tech companies or the companies that replace them will have to focus on building better products.

One example of a company that will have challenges is Amazon. They make a significant portion of their profits from AWS. As tech companies decline or go out of business, they will earn less. Also, their AWS infrastructure will become harder to maintain and people will move to competitors. Two other examples are Google and Meta, which make money from ads on websites that people could stop using.

Using the Internet in a healthy manner

I used to waste a large amount of time on the Internet. Usually, it was aimless browsing, although I would often spend a significant amount of time responding to forum posts. This included time spent thinking about what to post, or trying to figure out what I should say based on how people were responding to my post. Sometimes, this would involve also watching videos online. This section is a guide based on what I have learned to reduce excessive internet usage.

This section is not a guide on how to avoid the Internet. The Internet should not be seen as something harmful that should be avoided at all costs. People often have to use the Internet for work or personal reasons. People also enjoy using the Internet for various reasons such as playing games, watching funny videos, or connecting with others whom they are unable to see in person. I also started the first draft for this section using Google Docs, which meant using the Internet.

This is also not a guide on how to avoid specific websites. For example, I do not use Facebook. However, I know that others use Facebook to keep in touch with friends or family, and many small businesses see Facebook as an essential part of advertising or communicating with people. On the other hand, everyone doesn’t use the same set of websites I do, and they might find certain websites I use to be not worthwhile. For example, I do not use LinkedIn while some other people say it is useful. In the past when I had a LinkedIn account, I found it useful for keeping in touch with former colleagues and finding jobs. People in other fields may not find LinkedIn useful or they may find it useful for things besides the ones I use the site for. In summary, it is important to remember that the usefulness and entertainment level of a website is subjective.

When growing up, the Internet was a completely different place from where it is now. I started using the Internet regularly in the early 2000s for playing online Flash games and reading about video games. This guide is focused on adults, since that is where I have the most relevant experience, and I would be guessing about how to give advice for kids to avoid the Internet since my past experience isn’t very relevant.

Although it is theoretically possible to avoid the Internet, I do not recommend doing so. If you need to use the Internet for work or personal reasons, avoidance will be a huge problem. Also, complete avoidance is a problem if you end up having to use the Internet in the future, since you will not have practice with how to use the Internet in a healthy manner.

When trying to cut back on using the Internet, I found that simply trying to use it less was not a viable option. I noticed that I had to fill the time used by the Internet with something I wanted to do, and find something that satisfied the need in a better manner. If I decided to just stop using the Internet, I would start feeling directionless and unsure of what to do, which was not very helpful. There were times when I would try to sleep more or do more work instead of browsing which wasn’t enough to be a long term solution, because there was still this unsatisfied desire.

Reading at home helped somewhat, although having my phone or laptop nearby made it difficult. Reading, sleeping more if you aren’t getting enough sleep, or trying to catch up on work are good long term replacements for excessive Internet browsing. However, they were not helpful for me as ways to replace Internet browsing in the short term. Sometimes multiple activities are necessary to replace browsing. It may not be realistic to do a specific activity at a certain time, or you may only be interested in a specific activity for a certain amount of time each week.

I found that video games were often a good replacement for browsing. For me, it was also important that the game did not require looking at online guides, have multiplayer, or have in app purchases. In other words, it had to be something that I could play without an Internet connection. I’ve also played board games in person frequently, and I’ve found them to be a great way to avoid the Internet. When I’m playing a board game, I’m interacting with physical pieces and having conversations with other people I’m playing with, and using the Internet is a distraction from doing these things. I have used the Internet on my phone during a board game if there was a period of downtime when waiting for my turn. However, I noticed that using the Internet affected my ability to focus on the game or interact with others.

At board game events, there are periods of downtime at the beginning when waiting for people, or between games if multiple games are being played. While I had the option of opening my phone and browsing the Internet, I found that this wasn’t an appealing option because I could talk to the other people who were there. When meeting friends or family, or relatives and having conversations with them, my mind is present on the conversation, and I’m not drawn toward the Internet. Even during periods of downtime, when it isn’t rude to start browsing, browsing does not feel appealing.

I’ve also found that doing other projects I want to work on have been helpful. In the past, this has involved working on trying to design a board game, program a video game, or work on various website ideas.

Outdoor exercise has also been helpful. For me, outdoor exercise as a way of accomplishing recurring tasks has been helpful since I live in an urban area and I can walk to places. For example, instead of ordering groceries online, I will walk to a grocery store. Using the gym has also been a good alternative. If the distance isn’t too far, walking instead of taking a rideshare, Metro, or bus has been helpful. While I find that I can browse while walking, I find the process exhausting since I still need to maintain awareness of my surroundings, so I find that walking is an effective way of getting me to avoid using the Internet.

I’ve also found that being away from Internet usage triggers is helpful for avoiding excess Internet usage. One of the most common triggers for me is browsing the Internet, including when I have a specific reason I want to browse such as looking up reviews for a video game. This creates a snowball effect for me, which I will explain in a future chapter. Doing specific activities away from the Internet is one way I avoid triggers. For example, when I’m playing a board game in person, I am focused on the game and the people I’m playing the game with, so I don’t have a strong desire to browse.

I would sometimes play board games online, and that would be a trigger for browsing. After I was done playing the games, I was already in front of my computer with an open browser so switching to browsing was a straightforward process. If I’m away from my computer, I have to sit down, make sure my computer is on, log in, and then open a browser. Some people have told me that they prefer playing board games in person, and some people have a different preference. I’m mentioning this to emphasize the fact that triggers are subjective.

On the subject of triggers, the fact that triggers are subjective should be considered when taking advice on how to avoid Internet usage. When reading about triggers that other people avoid, consider if that same trigger is relevant to you.

I have also noticed that my desire to use the Internet increases based on how much I used the Internet in the past 24 hours. For example, if I wake up in the morning and immediately start browsing the Internet, it is harder for me to avoid browsing during the day. If I spend a lot of time browsing the Internet during the day, it is harder for me to avoid browsing in the evening and night, and this affects my ability to sleep. I think of this like a snowball representing my desire to use the Internet that rolls down a mountain and picks up snow.

This is not a process that is exclusive to using the Internet or specific people. Psychiatrists have also noticed that there is a process in the brain that is related with this neurotransmitter called dopamine. When waking up, you have a certain amount of dopamine, and doing things that release that dopamine feel good. Aimless Internet browsing releases a large amount of dopamine in a short period of time. Later in the day, you will have less dopamine, so you will be looking for activities that are better at releasing your remaining dopamine, and Internet browsing is a way to squeeze out that remaining dopamine. Trying to finish chores only releases a small amount of dopamine, and will release almost none if you used up most of the dopamine early. As a result, you will not feel a significant reward from finishing chores, and you will want to browse.

On the other hand, if you finish chores in the morning after you wake up, you will have sufficient dopamine such that finishing the chores will release enough dopamine to motivate. I have also noticed a similar effect over a longer period. If I am able to avoid the Internet for a day and I am doing something else that requires being away from the Internet, my desire to use the Internet starts going down. I’ve noticed this on vacations or going to a wedding. Even if there is some time when I’m not interacting with someone and I’m not doing something specific, I become less interested in using the Internet, and start browsing less.

I’ve also had conversations with people who are good at avoiding excessive Internet usage. I’ve noticed that they have been avoiding excessive Internet usage for a while, so they don’t have a habit of aimless browsing. Aimless browsing is also easier to avoid since it is a significant departure from their personality.

It is also important to understand that people may need to use sites that you perceive as not being worthwhile. I recall having a conversation with a college freshman about using social media, and I mentioned that I do not use Instagram. He mentioned that he saw Instagram as a necessity for social reasons, and then implied that he was not very happy about using the site.

While offline activities are helpful, it is important to not completely avoid technology and ignore any instances where it is useful. I typed this article on a computer, and did not write it by hand on paper. Also, I looked through online newspaper archives to do research for this article.
