Why bottom up urbanism is important for the success of large development projects

{{>authors/bharatPonnaluriHeading}}

Large development projects are an important part of a thriving urban area. However, the success of these projects is dependent on promoting bottom up urbanism through input from locals. Local goverment policies can play a key role in promoting bottom up urbanism

One example can be seen by Arlington County's transit-oriented development(TOD) policy for the Rosslyn-Ballston corridor.

Bottom up urbanism and development of the Rosslyn-Ballston corridor

Two key factors success of the Rosslyn-Ballston corridor in Arlington are the Metro and county policy combined with participation from locals.

The route to success started in the 1960s during planning for the Metro. Initially, the plan was to run the Orange Line of the Metro above ground in the median of the 66 highway. However, Arlington County worked with local residents to change plans so that the Orange Line would be underground between Rosslyn and Ballston.

Considering input from residents helps ensure that they will want to actively support the project. Meanwhile, top down developments without input from residents are likely to fail. Ignoring input with top-down developments means that people will not have a way to challenge problems that will negatively impact their neighborhoods. As a result, opposition to these top-down projects is deserved, regardless of how well-intentioned the projects may be.

Talking residents' input into consideration when building the Metro and promoting a TOD policy would be key to ensuring the success of TOD. The enthusiasm for the Metro would be seen in a 1968 referendum where 78% of voters supported a $54 million bond issue to support the DC Metro.

Arlington County would continue to promote a TOD policy that has turned the Rosslyn Ballston corridor into a resounding success. There are still underdeveloped spaces near Metro stations on the Rosslyn Ballston corridor and the success of Arlington's TOD policy show that the spaces should be developed.

Apartment rents in Arlington County near Metro stations have steadily risen over the past 10 years. While the rents are causing affordability challenges, they also prove that TOD is desirable and that there is a shortage of places in the US that follow TOD.

How Ballston shows that TOD promotes urban resiliency

The success of Arlington's TOD policy can be seen by Ballston's resiliency during the Covid-19 pandemic. Many people were avoiding the Metro, so restaurants along the Rosslyn-Ballston corridor had to rely on takeout orders from people living within walking distance. This was not an issue for Ballston, where local restaurants thrived due to takeout orders from residents. This was a stark contrast to many other neighborhoods where restaurants struggled.

The mixed success of the Crystal City Water Park

The Crystal City Water Park was recently renovated to be an outdoor food court, and its success has been mixed. It was created as top-down development that has struggled due to an inconsistent number of daily customers. Lack of climate control in the seating areas has limited its ability to be used due to rain, snow, heat, or cold weather. The high prices of food are also an issue.

On the other hand, allowing some amount of bottom up input has had a positive effect. Large restaurant chains have been discouraged.

Suggestions

1. More transparency into development plans done by private developers so that locals have things they can give their input on

2. More pop-ups such as the Connection that have been created with input from locals

3. Find ways to have locals be more closely involved such as Arlington County's Adopt-a-Park program
