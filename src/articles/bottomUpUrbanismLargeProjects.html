Why bottom-up urbanism is important for the success of large development projects

{{>authors/articleHeading name='Bharat Ponnaluri' date='April 15, 2026' imageLink='https://createthirdplaces.nyc3.cdn.digitaloceanspaces.com/people/Bharat_Picture.jpg' }}

Large development projects are an important part of a thriving urban area. However, the success of these projects is dependent on ensuring that locals can give their input.

One example can be seen by Arlington County Virginia's transit-oriented development(TOD) policy which transformed the Rosslyn-Ballston corridor from a streetcar suburb into a thriving urban area.

Bottom-up urbanism and development of the Rosslyn-Ballston corridor

Rosslyn-Ballston corridor. (Photo by Arlington County)

At the start of the 1950s, Arlington had a zoning ordinance that restricted development of most land to single-family developments with lot size requirements of 5000 square feet or more.

In 1957, Arlington identified revenue challenges caused the fact that people living in single-family homes contributed 48% of county revenue while requiring 68% of expenditures. To avoid tax hikes, Arlington rezoned neighborhoods to permit density increases, including ones along the Rosslyn-Ballston corridor.

Local advocacy and Arlington's TOD success

The route to TOD success started in the 1960s during planning for the Metro. Initially, the Orange Line of the Metro was planned to be above ground in Arlington in a highway median. However, the county decided to pursue TOD and worked with local residents to change plans so that the Orange Line would be underground between Rosslyn and Ballston. Later, planners worked with residents to develop a 'bull's eye' plan for density around the 5 Metro stations on the corridor.

Taking residents' input into consideration with TOD would be a key to its success. The enthusiasm for the Metro would be seen in a 1968 referendum where 78% of Arlington voters supported a $54 million bond issue to support the DC Metro. Arlington has also been careful to avoid upzoning areas near Metro stations with single family homeowners without their consent. This shows a consideration for residents, which is essential for a thriving urban area, and it has prevented significant opposition to TOD from homeowners.

Arlington County continued pursuing a TOD policy that has turned the Rosslyn Ballston corridor into a resounding success. According to a 1970 census, Arlington had around 30,000 detached single-family homes, and that number remained steady over the following 50 years. Meanwhile, the stock of other types of housing increased from around 41,000 units to 88,000 units, and the Arlington's population increased by around 60,000. Additional TOD density increases have also taken place along transit accessible areas on Columbia Pike, Pentagon City, and Crystal City.

TOD in Arlington also shows that density can be increased without more traffic. A study done in Arlington county from 1996 to 2012 showed traffic decreases along many roads. The steepest decline of 23% was from Wilson Boulevard, which is the main street running through the Rosslyn-Ballston corridor.

There are still underdeveloped commercials on the Rosslyn Ballston corridor and the success of Arlington's TOD policy show that they should be developed.

How Ballston shows that TOD promotes urban resiliency

Arlington's TOD policy was a key part of Ballston's resiliency during the Covid-19 pandemic Many people were avoiding the Metro, restaurants along the Rosslyn-Ballston corridor had to rely on takeout from people living nearby. During this time, local restaurants thrived due to takeout orders from residents.

Many apartments in Ballston are mixed-use developments with restaurants on the ground floor. I lived in one of these mixed use developments, and I enjoyed having a convenient food option nearby.

Ballston apartment building with ground floor restaurant. I lived on the third floor. (Photo by Bharat Ponnaluri)

Suggestions

1. Encourage collaboration between local government agencies and residents to develop public space

In 2016, Arlington opened the Connection library pop-up in the Crystal City Underground, after working with residents to develop the plans. Although the library was only planned to be open for 9 months, it remained open until the end of 2019, and support from Crystal City residents played a key role.

Connection Library in 2017. (Source: Wikimedia Commons)

The library was also a great example of adaptive reuse for a space in the Crystal City Underground that would have otherwise remained unused. It also helped Arlington's TOD policy because it was within close walking distance of the Crystal City Metro.

Recently, Arlington County started a program for library visitors to share library memories. At the Central Library, people could share their library memories. They were then posted on the wall for others to see.

Central Library Share a Favorite Library Memory. (Photo by Bharat Ponnaluri)

Another successful example can be seen by Arlington County's Adopt-a-Park program.

2. Support outdoor plazas

Welburn Square in Ballston. (Photo by Bharat Ponnaluri)

Outdoor plazas are a great place for local residents to gather. They help people connect and find ways to work together to support their neighborhoods. By being more connected with their neighborhoods, plazas will encourage people to care more about their neighborhoods. However, they should be combined with climate controlled indoor spaces.

Ballston plaza with restaurant seating. (Photo by Bharat Ponnaluri)

Plazas are also a great opportunity for restaurants to expand their space with some outdoor seating.

The Ellipse in Ballston. (Photo by Bharat Ponnaluri)

3. Support pop-up stores from small businesses

A pop-up store is a great starting point for a local business due to lower upfront investments and not having to pay rent for a regular storefront.

Pop-up art store in Ballston. (Photo by Bharat Ponnaluri)

4. Concentrate density increases in specific areas

Localities should concentrate density in specific areas instead of supporting scattered density increases. A TOD policy should be followed in urban areas. Smaller cities or town should concentrate density around a landmark such as central plaza or main street. Also, many people prefer lower density, and concentrated density encourages the preservation of these areas. Bottom up urbanism also means respecting different preferences for how people want to live.

Concentrating density in specific areas also means more space for public parks, which are great informal gathering spaces for supporting bottom-up urbanism.
