Why bottom up urbanism is important for the success of large development projects

{{>authors/bharatPonnaluriHeading}}

Large development projects are an important part of a thriving urban area. However, the success of these projects is dependent on promoting bottom up urbanism through input from locals. Local government policies can play a key role in promoting bottom up urbanism

One example can be seen by Arlington County's transit-oriented development(TOD) policy for the Rosslyn-Ballston corridor.

Bottom up urbanism and development of the Rosslyn-Ballston corridor

In Arlington, participation from locals was a key part of ensuring the success of the Metro and the county's TOD policy.

The route to success started in the 1960s during planning for the Metro. Initially, the plan was to run the Orange Line of the Metro above ground in the median of the 66 highway. However, Arlington County decided to pursue a TOD worked with local residents to change plans so that the Orange Line would be underground between Rosslyn and Ballston.

Considering input from residents helps ensure that they will want to actively support the project. Meanwhile, top down developments without input from residents are likely to fail. Ignoring input with top-down developments means that people will not have a way to challenge problems that will negatively impact their neighborhoods. As a result, opposition to these top-down projects is deserved, regardless of how well-intentioned the projects may be.

Talking residents' input into consideration when building the Metro and promoting a TOD policy would be key to ensuring the success of TOD. The enthusiasm for the Metro would be seen in a 1968 referendum where 78% of voters supported a $54 million bond issue to support the DC Metro.

Arlington County would continue to promote a TOD policy that has turned the Rosslyn Ballston corridor into a resounding success. There are still underdeveloped spaces near Metro stations on the Rosslyn Ballston corridor and the success of Arlington's TOD policy show that the spaces should be developed.

Apartment rents in Arlington County near Metro stations have steadily risen over the past 10 years. While the rents are causing affordability challenges, they also prove that TOD is desirable and that there is a shortage of places in the US that follow TOD.

How Ballston shows that TOD promotes urban resiliency

The success of Arlington's TOD policy can be seen by Ballston's resiliency during the Covid-19 pandemic. Many people were avoiding the Metro, so restaurants along the Rosslyn-Ballston corridor had to rely on takeout orders from people living within walking distance. This was not an issue for Ballston, where local restaurants thrived due to takeout orders from residents.

Many apartments in Ballston were build as mixed-use developments with restaurants on the ground floor. I lived in one of these mixed use developments, and I enjoyed having a convenient food option nearby.

Apartment in Ballston with restaurant on ground floor. I lived on the third floor. (Photo by Bharat Ponnaluri)

The mixed success of the Crystal City Water Park

The Crystal City Water Park was recently renovated to be an outdoor food court, and its success has been mixed. It was created as top-down development that has struggled due to an inconsistent number of daily customers. Lack of climate control in the seating areas has limited its ability to be used due to rain, snow, heat, or cold weather. The high prices of food are also an issue.

On the other hand, allowing some amount of bottom up input has had a positive effect. Large restaurant chains have been discouraged.

Crystal City Water Park on a Friday evening (Photo by Bharat Ponnaluri)

Suggestions

1.Encourage local government agencies to work with locals to develop and improve public space.

In 2016, Arlington opened the Connection library pop-up in the Crystal City Underground. Arlington worked with Crystal City residents to develop the plans. Although the library was only planned to be open for 9 months, it remained open until the end of 2019, and support from Crystal City residents played a key role.

Connection Library in 2017. (Source: Wikimedia Commons)

The library was also a great example of adaptive reuse for a space in the Crystal City Underground that would have otherwise remained unused. It also helped Arlington's TOD policy because it was within close walking distance of the Crystal City Metro.

Recently, Arlington County started a program for library visitors to share library memories. At the Central Library, people could share their library memories. They were then posted on the wall for others to see.

Central Library Share a Favorite Library Memory. (Photo by Bharat Ponnaluri)

Another successful example can be seen by Arlington County's Adopt-a-Park program.

2. Support outdoor plazas

Welburn Square in Ballston. (Photo by Bharat Ponnaluri)

Outdoor plazas are a great place for local residents to gather. They help people connect and find ways to work together to support their neighborhoods. By being more connected with their neighborhoods, plazas will encourage people to care more about their neighborhoods. However, they should be combined with climate controlled indoor spaces.

Ballston plaza with restaurant seating. (Photo by Bharat Ponnaluri)

Plazas are also a great opportunity for restaurants to expand their space with some outdoor seating.

The Ellipse in Ballston. (Photo by Bharat Ponnaluri)

3. Support pop-up stores from small businesses

A pop-up store is a great starting point for a local business due to lower upfront investments and not having to pay rent for a regular storefront.

Pop-up art store in Ballston. (Photo by Bharat Ponnaluri)

4. More transparency into development plans done by private developers so that locals have things they can give their input on
